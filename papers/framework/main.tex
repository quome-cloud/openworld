{\sffamily\bfseries\color{cardblue} ABSTRACT}\par
\smallskip
\fontsize{9.0}{10.7}\selectfont
We introduce \textbf{world computing}: the discipline of building and operating \textbf{world
computers}---the combination of hardware and software that instantiates, verifies, composes, and
runs \emph{simulated worlds with verifiable dynamics}. Just as a general-purpose computer makes
\emph{computation} a cheap, composable, inspectable commodity, a world computer aims to do the
same for \emph{worlds}. We present \openworld{}, a zero-dependency software substrate for world
computing, and study the two questions a new substrate must answer: how cheaply can worlds be
\emph{prototyped}, and how well can they be \emph{evaluated}? We answer both by benchmarking the
world computer the way one benchmarks any computer---on \textbf{cost}, \textbf{latency}, and
\textbf{performance}. \textbf{Prototyping (cost \& latency).} A world is \emph{declared} and its
dynamics \emph{synthesized as code and verified before acceptance} (a plan--generate--verify
relay over syntax, sandboxed behaviour, declared invariants, and an optional critic); on a
\ProtoNumWorlds-world, six-sector benchmark an agent (Claude Code) authors structurally-validated,
servable worlds---well-formed and runnable, a \emph{conformance} rate, not certified
rule-correctness---at a median of \ProtoMedianMinutes{} minutes each. The substrate is
zero-dependency, the worlds it builds run on \emph{consumer hardware}, and transition synthesis
itself succeeds with local 7B--9B models (no training); only the authoring \emph{agent} is a
frontier model. Once built, a verified world is plain code that rolls out
at native speed (orders of magnitude faster than a per-step LLM, and inspectable). \textbf{Evaluation
(performance \& trust).} We measure what makes a world trustworthy: validation and servability
gates, exact-rollout fidelity with no compounding
error, compositional correctness (a recursive \texttt{CompositeWorld} algebra with verified
bridges and aggregators that defeats the monolithic-synthesis complexity cliff), a symmetric
perceive$\to$world$\to$emit boundary that adds zero dynamics error, open inference-time value
dials, and \emph{portable specs + model cards} that
round-trip losslessly so a world can be audited before it is trusted (a secondary
\emph{agents-as-a-judge} selector is measured but only marginally helpful). We exercise the substrate
across many domains---economies, relativity, trading, brain simulators, optimal-transport drift
detection---to show its expressiveness. \textbf{Scope:} the symbolic-state regime, where dynamics are writable; pixel-native
domains remain the territory of learned models. What these worlds \emph{enable} as training
signal---\emph{world-time compute}---is the subject of a companion paper; here the worlds, the
hardware that runs them, and their evaluation are the object of study. All code, the
\ProtoNumWorlds{} recipes, and this manuscript regenerate from one repository.

\section{Introduction}
\textbf{World computing.} A general-purpose computer is hardware and software for processing
\emph{programs over data}; we propose the analogous discipline for processing \emph{worlds}.
Define a \textbf{world computer} as the combination of hardware and software that instantiates,
verifies, composes, and runs \emph{simulated worlds with verifiable dynamics}---worlds whose
transition function is executable, checkable code rather than learned weights---and
\textbf{world computing} as the study and practice of building and operating them. Where
classical computing made \emph{computation} a cheap, composable, inspectable commodity, world
computing aims to do the same for \emph{simulated worlds}: author a world in minutes, verify its
dynamics before trusting them, compose worlds into larger worlds, serve them as live services,
and share them as portable, auditable artifacts. This paper presents \openworld{}, a
zero-dependency software substrate for world computing that turns authoring a verified world
model (perceive\,$\to$\,world\,$\to$\,emit\,$\to$\,act, served and shareable) into a
minutes-scale, push-button activity (\S\ref{sec:prototyping}) on consumer hardware. The unit it
processes is a \emph{world model}: a simulator that, given a state and an action, predicts the
next state, letting an agent plan ``in imagination'' rather than by trial~\cite{ha2018world}.

\textbf{Why verified code.} A world computer's worlds must not lie: their value---for planning,
for analysis, or as the training signal studied in the companion paper---depends on their
dynamics being \emph{correct}. So \openworld{} builds dynamics as \emph{verified code},
synthesized and then checked before acceptance, rather than as learned approximations: exact
over long rollouts with no compounding error and---being plain code---running orders of magnitude
faster than a per-step LLM (Figure~\ref{fig:worldcomputer}B). PyTorch did not give deep
learning new models; it made them fast to \emph{prototype}, and an explosion followed---world
models have lacked that tool. One application we highlight is \emph{manufacturing training
experience}: because a verified world labels trajectories exactly for free, fine-tuning on
trajectories from many worlds of a domain (\emph{world-time compute}) lifts a model's
generalization to held-out worlds, the subject of the companion paper~\cite{openworld_wtc}. But
a world computer is useful whether or not one ever fine-tunes.

\textbf{Evaluating a world computer.} A substrate is only as good as its measured economics, so
we treat \openworld{} as a system to benchmark on the three axes one benchmarks any
computer---\emph{cost} (compute and wall-clock to author and to run a world, on consumer
hardware), \emph{latency} (time-to-first-validated-world, and per-step rollout time), and
\emph{performance} (validation and servability rates, exact-rollout fidelity, and compositional
correctness). The paper is organized around these: prototyping cost and latency
(\S\ref{sec:prototyping}), then the components and evaluation that establish performance and
trust, then a breadth of domain demonstrations that probe the substrate's expressiveness.

A world model is an agent's internal simulator: given a state and an action, it
predicts the next state (and/or observation), letting the agent plan ``in
imagination'' rather than by trial in the world~\cite{ha2018world}. That
\emph{genus} is shared across the field; research programs diverge on \emph{how the
model represents state and how it is obtained}, and the term today names two largely
disjoint \emph{species}. The dominant, most visible one is \emph{perceptual and
generative}: a neural network learned from large amounts of data whose state is a
continuous latent---or pixels, video, or a 3D scene---and whose output is a
\emph{generated observation}. It runs from the V--M--C triad of Ha and
Schmidhuber~\cite{ha2018world} through the RSSM-based Dreamer
family~\cite{dreamerv3}, value-equivalent planning in MuZero~\cite{muzero},
autoregressive token models such as IRIS~\cite{iris2023} and
$\Delta$-IRIS~\cite{deltairis2024}, and diffusion dynamics in
DIAMOND~\cite{diamond2024}, joint-embedding predictive video models that
forecast in representation space such as V-JEPA~\cite{vjepa2025}; and, most
prominently in recent generative AI, to interactive generative environments and
``spatial intelligence''---internet-scale video and 3D world generators such as
Genie~\cite{genie2024}, video-generation-as-simulator (Sora)~\cite{sora2024}, and
Fei-Fei Li's World Labs~\cite{worldlabs2025}. Powerful as these are, they share three structural
costs: they are \emph{data-hungry} (millions of environment steps, or
internet-scale video), \emph{compounding} (every learned transition adds error
that accumulates over a rollout), and \emph{opaque} (dynamics and values alike
live in continuous weights that cannot be inspected, edited, or audited).

\paragraph{What ``world model'' means in this paper.} Because the term has come to
connote those generative, pixel- and 3D-native systems, we state our usage plainly.
We use \emph{world model} in its original, narrower sense---a forward predictor of
the next \emph{state} for planning---and we restrict the state to a \emph{symbolic}
object: a typed record of named variables (counts, prices, positions, flags), not
pixels, latents, or 3D geometry. Our world models are therefore the second species,
\emph{Code World Models} (CWMs): the transition is an explicit, human-readable
\emph{program} over that symbolic state, synthesized by an LLM from a declared rule
set and verified before use, and a rollout returns the next symbolic state
\emph{exactly} rather than a generated image, with \emph{zero} training data. This
is the same genus as the perceptual models above (a simulator the agent rolls
forward) but a different species---and an intentionally complementary one.
OpenWorld targets domains that can be \emph{specified} symbolically (inventories,
markets, schedules, negotiations, physics with known equations); it is \emph{not} a
pixel-native, perceptual, or open-world-3D model and does not generate images,
video, or scenes. Where the spatial-intelligence systems \emph{learn what cannot be
told}, OpenWorld \emph{writes down what can be told, and verifies it}; the two are
better read as adjacent tools than as competitors.

\paragraph{World, action, agent, simulation---and why a \emph{verified} model is
special.} The world model is only one of four distinct objects, and keeping them
separate dissolves much of the remaining confusion. A \emph{world} is the
environment-as-simulator: a symbolic state, a discrete set of \emph{actions}, and
the verified transition that maps a $(\textrm{state}, \textrm{action})$ pair to the
next state (plus optional perception/emit boundaries). An \emph{agent} is a
\emph{separate} policy---an LLM planner or a fixed rule---that chooses actions
toward a goal; it is a \emph{user} of a world, not a part of it, so one world serves
any agent and a planner can be swapped without re-synthesizing dynamics. A
\emph{simulation} closes the loop: it places agents in a world and repeatedly
observes, chooses an action, and steps the world, producing a trajectory scored by
objectives. The textbook definition puts the world model \emph{inside} the agent
(the simulator it plans with) and distinguishes it from the true environment; the
gap between the two---model bias, sim-to-real error---is exactly what bounds learned
world models. A verified code world model does not so much eliminate that gap as
\emph{relocate} it: once the dynamics are declared symbolically and the accepted
program reproduces them exactly, the agent's internal model and the (symbolic)
environment are the same artifact, so the dynamics contribute \emph{no} rollout
error---what error remains lives entirely in the \emph{specification} and in
\emph{perception} (the symbol-grounding boundary, \S\ref{sec:perception}),
not in the transition. This is why the same \texttt{World} serves, without
modification, both as the ground-truth environment and as the model an agent plans
through in our planning experiments; it is also why this paper is scoped to
\emph{specifiable} domains, where that relocation is a genuine win.

This Code World Model bargain---programs instead of weights---buys verifiability,
compute-cheap rollouts,
and out-of-distribution generalization by construction~\cite{worldcoder2024,
gifmcts2024, poeworld2025, cwmgames2025}. Yet CWM research has remained a set
of bespoke systems aimed at particular benchmarks. There has been no general,
lightweight framework that lets a practitioner \emph{declare} a world, have a
local model write and verify its dynamics, steer behavior through declared
value weights, and optimize the whole configuration against a goal---the
workflow that made supervised learning ubiquitous.

\openworld{} is that framework, and this paper benchmarks the paradigm it
operationalizes against the learned-dynamics alternative. The framework
contributes four mechanisms: (i) a \emph{plan--generate--verify relay} in
which a generator LLM writes transition code that must survive syntactic
checks, sandboxed smoke-runs, declared invariants, and optionally a
second-model critic before acceptance; (ii) \emph{open value
specification}---objectives are scoring functions weighted by tunable dials,
so moral configuration is an editable artifact rather than a frozen weight,
directly addressing the specification trap~\cite{spectrap2025}; (iii) an
\emph{automated tuner} that searches world design, policy knobs, and moral
dials jointly (random, local-refinement, and Optuna TPE~\cite{optuna2019}
strategies); and (iv) \emph{agents-as-a-judge}~\cite{agentasjudge2024,
llmjudge2023}: an LLM judge that selects among candidate behaviors and grades
trajectories against written rubrics.

A review of the world-model landscape reveals distinct strategy families with
a recurring tension---fidelity and scale on one side; verifiability, sample
efficiency, and steerability on the other (Table~\ref{tab:related}).

\paragraph{Contributions.} This paper develops \emph{world computing}---building, evaluating,
and serving verified world models as a first-class artifact---organized around a world
computer's cost, latency, and performance (Figure~\ref{fig:worldcomputer}):
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{World computing and the world computer.} We frame a discipline: a \emph{world
  computer} is the combination of hardware and software that instantiates, verifies, composes,
  and runs simulated worlds with verifiable dynamics. We make the abstraction precise---worlds are
  closed under a verification-preserving \emph{composition algebra} (\S\ref{sec:algebra}: closure,
  identity, associativity, and compositional verification)---and build \openworld, a
  zero-dependency software substrate for it.
  \item \textbf{The substrate (framework).} A local LLM (or Claude Code) \emph{declares} a
  world---symbolic state, a verified \texttt{transition}, perceptors (the perceive boundary),
  composition (worlds within worlds), dial-steerable objectives, an emit/act boundary---which a
  plan--generate--verify relay checks before acceptance, then serves from one command and shares
  as a portable artifact.
  \item \textbf{Prototyping cost \& latency.} On a \ProtoNumWorlds-world benchmark across
  \ProtoNumSectors{} regulated sectors (healthcare, financial services, legal, cybersecurity,
  energy, agentic-AI), an agent (Claude Code) authors every world at a median of \ProtoMedianMinutes{} minutes
  (p90 \ProtoPNinetyMinutes, max \ProtoMaxMinutes); the substrate and the worlds run on consumer
  hardware and transition synthesis works with local 7B--9B models (E2/E16), with no datacenter
  training; all \ProtoNumWorlds{} ship as runnable recipes (Figure~\ref{fig:world-atlas}).
  \item \textbf{Runtime latency.} Once built, a verified world is plain code: it rolls out at
  native speed---orders of magnitude faster than a per-step LLM next-state proxy---exactly and
  inspectably (Figure~\ref{fig:worldcomputer}B), so the one-time synthesis cost is amortized over
  unlimited fast, exact rollouts.
  \item \textbf{Evaluation \& trust.} \ProtoValidationRate{} pass a structural
  \texttt{validate\_spec} gate and \ProtoExecutableRate{} clear a behavioral audit; verified
  synthesized dynamics are exact on \MultiCodeExact{} \RolloutSteps-step rollouts (95\% CI
  \MultiCodeCI) and answer $10\times$ out-of-distribution probes at \CodeOODRate; composition is
  verified compositionally and defeats the monolithic-synthesis complexity cliff; and the
  perceive$\to$world$\to$emit boundary adds zero dynamics error. Verification is itself measured:
  each gate's contribution is ablated ($+0.24$ probe accuracy over blind acceptance), and we are
  explicit that it certifies executable, invariant-respecting code, \emph{not} rule-correctness
  (on a weak 3B generator the gate accepts every program yet none is branch-exact---a 100\%
  branch-level false-accept rate).
  \item \textbf{Shareable, auditable worlds.} A portable spec plus a generated model card
  round-trips losslessly, so a world can be published and checked before it is trusted.
  \item \textbf{Breadth \& reproducibility.} We exercise the substrate across a wide range of
  domains (economies, corporate organizations, relativity, trading, brain simulators, optimal
  transport) and ship \NumExperiments{} seed-fixed experiments regenerable from one repository.
\end{enumerate}
What these worlds \emph{enable} as training signal---\emph{world-time compute}---is the subject
of the companion paper~\cite{openworld_wtc}.

\paragraph{Positioning in the field.} Two methodologies build \emph{code} world models. The
\emph{neural} route trains the simulator \emph{into weights}---Meta FAIR's CWM~\cite{metacwm2025}
mid-trains a 32B model on Python-execution and Docker-container traces, then applies multi-task
RL, at a cost of weeks on multi-H100 infrastructure ($\sim$160\,GB VRAM)---yielding a powerful but
opaque, GPU-bound simulator whose dynamics are not runnable code. The \emph{programmatic} route
instead has an LLM \emph{write} the dynamics as a verified program (WorldCoder, GIF-MCTS,
PoE-World~\cite{worldcoder2024,gifmcts2024,poeworld2025}); generation takes seconds-to-minutes and
the result then rolls out at compiled speed, exactly. \openworld{} is the programmatic route at
\emph{substrate} scale: where prior code-world-model work hand-builds one simulator for one task,
world computing makes authoring, verifying, composing, serving, and
sharing verified worlds a routine, minutes-scale activity on consumer hardware---the cost,
latency, and auditability advantages over the neural route are precisely what this paper measures. It is complementary to, not in
competition with, the perceptual/generative world-model species (Dreamer, MuZero, Genie, Sora,
V-JEPA; Table~\ref{tab:related}), which learn pixel/latent dynamics from data: those win where
dynamics must be \emph{learned} from perception, world computing where they can be \emph{written}.
What such worlds \emph{enable} as training signal (\emph{world-time compute}) and how that relates
to self-training is developed in the companion paper~\cite{openworld_wtc}. The supporting machinery extends the open-specification
stance of the value-alignment literature~\cite{spectrap2025,mas2025} and judge-based
behavior selection~\cite{agentasjudge2024}. We benchmark on consumer hardware with 7B/3B
local models, the regime where learned world models are least accessible and training-free
approaches matter most.

\section{Problem Setting and Positioning}\label{sec:problem}
We target symbolic-state environments: worlds whose state is a
JSON-serializable structure, whose action set is discrete, and whose dynamics
follow declarable rules. This covers operational simulations (triage,
negotiation, portfolios, sprints) and program repair, but not pixel-native
domains; we return to this boundary in the limitations. The practitioner
declares a world (state schema, actions, plain-language rules), and the
substrate must produce a faithful, fast, auditable simulator from it. The
adversary here is not a security attacker but \emph{model error itself}:
hallucinated transitions, silently wrong code, and value drift.

\paragraph{The object we build.} Formally, an \openworld{} world model is a tuple
$W = (\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, s_0, \tau, \{(o_i, w_i)\}_i, \mathcal{I})$ of
symbolic states, a discrete action set, an initial state, a \emph{deterministic
transition program} $\tau$, weighted objectives, and invariants $\mathcal{I}$
(stochasticity is modeled as replayable seeded determinism, so $\tau$ stays a
pure function). The practitioner declares everything but $\tau$, which the
substrate \emph{produces} from the natural-language rules by \emph{synthesis
under verification}---an LLM proposes a program that is accepted only if it
parses, executes in a sandbox respecting every invariant, and (optionally)
survives a second-model critique. Because the accepted $\tau$ is then \emph{fixed
code}, a rollout is computed by executing it, so it is exact at every depth with
no compounding error---unlike a learned, per-step-resampled transition whose
exactness decays as $(1-\epsilon)^t$. We give the full formal definition and the
compounding-error analysis in the companion paper~\cite{openworld_wtc}; this
paper takes the verified-code world model as its object and studies how to
\emph{prototype, evaluate, and serve} it at scale---i.e.\ as a world computer.

world-model strategy families surveyed in the accompanying literature review.

\section{System Design}
\subsection{Overview and components}
\openworld{} composes six unit-testable components: \texttt{World} (declared
state ontology, actions, rules, and a pluggable transition engine); three
interchangeable dynamics engines (\texttt{CodeTransition}: synthesized,
verified, executable Python; \texttt{LLMTransition}: per-step LLM next-state
prediction, our learned-style baseline; \texttt{FunctionTransition}:
hand-written oracle); \texttt{Agent} (LLM planner or deterministic policy);
\texttt{Objective}+\texttt{Dial} (open value specification);
\texttt{Simulation} (scored trajectories); \texttt{Tuner} (configuration
search); and \texttt{Judge} (candidate selection and rubric grading). The
backbone is any model served by Ollama, reached through the Python standard
library; the framework has zero runtime dependencies.

\subsection{A declarative world specification}\label{sec:language}

\begin{tcolorbox}[policycard, cardcol=cardblue,
  title=A world declaration and its verified compilation, width=0.94\linewidth]

)                                    # accepted code is a plain, editable artifact

\subsection{The plan--generate--verify relay}
\texttt{compile()} prompts a generator model with the world context and
demands a single pure function \texttt{transition(state, action)}. Each
candidate passes three gates: (1) \emph{syntactic}---the code parses and
defines the required function; (2) \emph{behavioral}---it executes in a
restricted sandbox (curated builtins; no imports or I/O; inputs deep-copied so
candidate code can never mutate caller state) against every declared action
and must satisfy the world's invariants; (3) \emph{semantic}
(optional)---a second model reviews the code against the written rules and
returns \texttt{PASS} or concrete feedback. Any failure is appended to the
next generation prompt; the loop is bounded and returns the full attempt
history on failure. Accepted dynamics are plain source: they can be diffed
against intent, unit-tested, edited, saved, and reloaded.

\subsection{Open value specification and configuration search}
Objectives are named scoring functions over $(s, a, s')$ weighted by floats or
\texttt{Dial}s---tunable scalars adjustable mid-simulation. Trajectories
record each objective's raw series, so dial sweeps trace true Pareto frontiers
rather than scalarized aggregates. The \texttt{Tuner} treats world parameters,
policy thresholds, and dials as one search space (dials are first-class:
passed directly, tuned over their declared bounds), evaluates each candidate
configuration by simulation, and supports broad random search, Optuna
TPE~\cite{optuna2019} for expensive worlds, and hill-climbing refinement
around incumbents.

\subsection{Agents-as-a-judge}
Following the agent-as-a-judge line~\cite{agentasjudge2024,llmjudge2023}, a
\texttt{Judge} wraps any backbone model with two operations:
\texttt{choose(options, context)}---select among $N$ candidate actions,
patches, or plans---and \texttt{score\_trajectory(trajectory,
rubric)}---grade an episode 0--10 against a written rubric. Unparseable
verdicts degrade to safe defaults. Judges plug into action selection
(\S\ref{sec:judge-results}) and can serve as objectives where no programmatic
score exists.

\subsection{A composition algebra for worlds}\label{sec:algebra}
What makes \openworld{} a \emph{world computer} rather than a collection of simulators is that
worlds are closed under a verification-preserving composition algebra---worlds are the values, and
composition is the operation that builds bigger worlds from smaller ones. Write $\mathcal{W}$ for
the set of world models $W=(\mathcal{S},\mathcal{A},s_0,\tau,\{(o_i,w_i)\},\mathcal{I})$ defined in
\S\ref{sec:problem}. Given children $\{W_k\}_{k=1}^{n}\subset\mathcal{W}$ under distinct namespace
keys, \emph{bridges} $\mathcal{B}$ (each an ordinary transition over the two-slot state of a coupled
pair, synthesized and verified by the \emph{same} gate as any transition), \emph{aggregators}
$\mathcal{G}$ (pure functions of the children's states), and downward \emph{bindings}, composition
produces $C=\mathrm{compose}(\{W_k\},\mathcal{B},\mathcal{G})$: a world whose state
$\mathcal{S}_C=\prod_k \mathcal{S}_k$ (with a derived aggregate slot) nests each child's state under
its key, whose actions $\mathcal{A}_C=\bigsqcup_k \mathcal{A}_k$ address children by namespace, and
whose transition $\tau_C$ executes, in fixed order: (i)~downward bindings, (ii)~the addressed
child's own $\tau_k$ on its slice (children run \emph{unmodified}), (iii)~the bridges, (iv)~a
recompute of the aggregators; invariants lift as $\mathcal{I}_C=\bigcup_k\mathcal{I}_k\cup\mathcal{I}_{\mathcal{B}}$.

\paragraph{Closure, identity, associativity.} $C\in\mathcal{W}$: \textrm{compose} returns a world,
so its output can itself be a child of a further composition---worlds-of-worlds nest to arbitrary
depth (the implementation makes \texttt{CompositeWorld} a subclass of \texttt{World}). A single-child
composite with no bridges is the child up to its namespace wrapper, $\mathrm{compose}(\{W\})\cong W$
(\emph{identity}); and because $\tau_C$ acts slice-wise with bridges local to the pair they couple,
$\mathrm{compose}(\mathrm{compose}(W_1,W_2),W_3)\cong\mathrm{compose}(W_1,\mathrm{compose}(W_2,W_3))$
up to renaming of namespace keys (\emph{associativity}). Composition is therefore a well-defined
algebra on $\mathcal{W}$, not a one-off wrapper.

\paragraph{Verification is compositional---and that is the point.} If every child $\tau_k$ and every
bridge transition is accepted by the synthesis-under-verification gate, then $\tau_C$ is verified
\emph{by construction}: it only ever invokes already-verified pieces, in the fixed order above, over
disjoint namespace slices, so the whole needs no monolithic re-synthesis or re-verification.
Composition thus turns an $n$-rule synthesis problem---which a single generator handles reliably
only up to a small rule count before a \emph{complexity cliff}---into $n$ small, independently
verified problems plus local bridges. This is the algebraic content behind the empirical result that
\emph{composition defeats the cliff} (\S\ref{sec:composition}), and the spec serializes a composite
recursively (\S\ref{sec:specs}), so the algebra is also the unit of sharing. A substrate with a
closed, verification-preserving composition operator over a well-defined value type is what earns
the name \emph{world computer}.

\subsection{Verified reward induction: the objective as a first-class artifact}\label{sec:reward-induction}
The relay synthesizes and verifies the \emph{dynamics} as code, but a world model is only half of an
agent: the \emph{objective} has so far been \emph{declared} (a hand-written scoring function), not
\emph{discovered}. For interactive problems where the goal itself is unknown---an agent dropped into
an unfamiliar game must infer what counts as success---we make the objective symmetric to the
transition: a \emph{verified reward as code}. A \texttt{CodeObjective} carries a sandboxed
\texttt{reward(state, action, next\_state)} (a scalar; a goal predicate is just a $0/1$ reward) that
runs in the same pure-Python sandbox as \texttt{CodeTransition}, and \texttt{induce\_reward}
synthesizes it from observed reward transitions, accepting it only when it \emph{exact-matches}
held-out rewards---the same plan--generate--verify gate, now applied to the objective rather than the
dynamics. This closes the loop: verified \emph{perception} (\S\ref{sec:perception}) $+$ verified
\emph{dynamics} $+$ verified \emph{reward} $+$ planning, with every component synthesized and
exact-match verified. It is the foundational primitive that interactive benchmarks such as ARC-AGI-3
demand, where the central difficulty is \emph{goal discovery}, not dynamics (companion work): once a
single positive reward is observed, the win condition is induced as verified code and becomes
plannable. Two further foundational primitives follow the same symmetry and are now implemented:
\emph{typed-object perceptors} (\texttt{make\_typed\_perceptor}---a sandboxed \texttt{CodePerceptor}
that segments and role-labels objects, agent/target/counter, so goals are expressed over structure
not pixels) and a \emph{consensus world} (\texttt{ConsensusTransition}---an aggregator over several
independently synthesized world models that selects the best-verified member by default, since
averaging weak correlated code models empirically hurts). \emph{Composite objectives} (the
composition algebra applied to goals, decomposing a win into verified sub-goals) remain the open
extension. Together, perception, dynamics, and reward are each a verified code artifact, and the
committee and the typed perceptor are verification-aware---the framework's coverage of the agent loop
is now closed end-to-end.

\subsection{Implementation}
Python ($\geq$3.9). The core is \emph{zero-dependency}: \texttt{import
openworld} and everything it pulls in (state, transitions, composition, specs,
cards, perception) uses only the standard library, while the numpy-backed
analysis extras (wavelets, information geometry, optimal transport) load lazily
and the optional Optuna tuner and serving/CLI layer are never imported by the
core. The codebase is $\sim$\LibLOC{} lines across \LibModules{} core modules
with \LibTests{} unit-test functions that run fully offline via a scripted mock
backbone, and a regression test asserts the core import stays
standard-library-only. For interoperability, a world also exports a \emph{Gymnasium adapter}
(\texttt{openworld.gym\_env.OpenWorldEnv}, an optional extra that keeps the core stdlib-only):
it wraps a verified world as a standard \texttt{gym.Env} (\texttt{reset}/\texttt{step}, a
\texttt{Discrete} action space over the declared actions, a \texttt{Box} observation over the
numeric state), dropping a verified world into existing planners and RL libraries with rollouts
at native code speed rather than LLM inference. Released with worked examples, four domain
tutorials, the three instrumented worlds, the \NumTasks-task coding benchmark, and every
experiment script used here.

OpenWorld also supports \emph{causal DAGs} as a world-specification modality---a DAG compiles to a verified structural causal model with built-in assumption testing, demonstrated on a social-determinants-of-health world (E64); see Appendix~\ref{sec:dag}.

\section{Prototyping code world models}\label{sec:prototyping}
The bottleneck is not ideas but iteration speed: a faithful simulator is a
multi-day-to-multi-week build, so most teams never make one. OpenWorld removes that
bottleneck. A world model is
a small declarative spec---symbolic state, a verified \texttt{transition}, optional
perceptors (the perceive boundary), composition (worlds within worlds, joined by
bridges and rolled up by aggregators), declared objectives steerable by dials, and an
emit/act boundary---and every piece is a few lines you write or that Claude Code writes
for you (Figure~\ref{fig:prototyping}). The same spec then serves: \texttt{openworld
serve} exposes it as a stateless inference API with a live view.

artifact is verified by construction rather than hoped-correct. This is the paper's
practical thesis: when dynamics are writable, prototyping a \emph{correct} world model is a
minutes-scale, push-button activity, and the rest of the paper establishes that the
resulting models are exact, composable, and steerable.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\paragraph{Worlds with ground truth.} Three deterministic oracle worlds
instrument every dynamics comparison: \emph{sprint} (backlog/debt/bug
dynamics with an integer-division interaction), \emph{orchard} (resource pool
with per-agent tallies), and \emph{triage} (queues, a clock, and
deterioration events). Oracles are hand-written
\texttt{FunctionTransition}s; synthesized and learned-style engines are
compared against them exactly, including on a fixed probe suite that
exercises clamping, empty-queue, and interaction branches.

\paragraph{The coding world.} A second symbolic world---program repair---exercises the
substrate's verification and judge machinery: each of \NumTasks{} tasks is a buggy Python
function plus a hidden test suite over classic defect archetypes, and the world's transition
\emph{is} test execution (a submitted patch runs bit-exactly in a sandbox with a wall-clock
alarm, and failing-test feedback enters the state for the next attempt). It is the substrate
that matters here, not a repair leaderboard; benchmark-scale (SWE-bench-style) repair suites
ship in the repository (\texttt{datasets/openworld-repairbench/}).

\paragraph{Composition, nesting, and changing rules (E30--E32).} Three
experiments exercise \texttt{CompositeWorld}, a wrapper whose state nests its
children's states under namespace keys. Children run \emph{unmodified}: the
composite slices a child's namespace out, steps the child's own transition,
and writes it back. Coupling is explicit---sideways through \texttt{Bridge}s
(an ordinary transition over the two-slot dict of the coupled children's
states, so the same synthesis-and-verification pipeline applies to bridges
unchanged), upward through \texttt{Aggregator}s (derived from leaves, never
simulated), and agents travel between children over \texttt{Route}s whose
crossing effects (tolls, vetoes) are themselves verifiable transitions.
\texttt{PhasedTransition} handles rules that change over time: ordered
(trigger, transition) phases with sequential, irreversible advance, every
phase constructed and verified \emph{before} the run. E30 holds one system
fixed---\CompRules{} internal rules plus four ring couplings---and varies
only how synthesis is posed: one monolithic prompt over a flat namespaced
state versus eight small prompts (four \CompChildRules-rule sectors, four
one-rule bridges), each verified independently and assembled into a
\texttt{CompositeWorld}; both conditions score on the same ground-truth probe
suite (7B generator, three replicates each). E31 replays a \NestSteps-step
script (leaf actions, ticks, and two travel attempts across a toll route) on
a three-level composite against an independent flat-dict oracle that imports
nothing from the composition machinery, checking leaf exactness, aggregator
consistency, money conservation, and agent-registry agreement at every step.
E32 declares a market whose policy changes at a fixed step mid-rollout and
compares phased synthesis (each phase from its own rule text alone),
monolithic synthesis from the combined rules-with-change text, and the LLM
next-state proxy, on closed-loop rollouts crossing the boundary.

\paragraph{Models and hardware.} All experiments use local models served by
Ollama on an Apple-silicon laptop: \texttt{qwen2.5:7b} (generator, judge,
critic in E3), \texttt{qwen2.5:3b} (small generator in E2/E3),
\texttt{qwen2.5:1.5b} (repair agent in E5/E6/E13/E17), and---for
cross-family replication (E16)---\texttt{llama3.1:8b} (Meta) and
\texttt{gemma2:9b} (Google). No cloud APIs and no fine-tuning---consumer
hardware throughout, which is the point: a world computer that runs where its
users are.

\paragraph{Baselines and latency.} For runtime, the comparator is
\texttt{LLMTransition}: the same backbone predicting each next state directly
from the same description and rules---sharing the symbolic engine's knowledge
but with the per-step inference structure of a learned model. It serves as the
\emph{latency} baseline (per-step rollout throughput, steps/s, E04) and the
fidelity baseline (it accumulates error over a rollout). We do not reimplement
the pixel-native learned family (DreamerV3-class systems require millions of
environment steps~\cite{dreamerv3}); we compare against it on \emph{properties}
(Table~\ref{tab:related}), not a laptop-scale retrain.

\paragraph{Metrics and statistics.} Rate metrics carry 95\% Wilson confidence
intervals. Dynamics fidelity uses exact-state match (every field equal),
first-divergence step, and final-state $L^1$ error; runtime latency is per-step
rollout throughput (steps/s). The judge selection comparison (E13) is a paired
design on shared candidate sets with an exact two-sided McNemar test, and judge
alignment uses Spearman rank correlation with permutation p-values (10{,}000
shuffles). Seeds are fixed in each script; every number in this paper
regenerates via \texttt{python3 scripts/make\_paper\_assets.py}.

\paragraph{Calibration, pilots, and internal review.} Design changes made
after pilots are reported rather than hidden: (a) the verifier ablation (E3)
uses the 3B generator at high temperature because the 7B generator's first
attempts passed every gate, leaving nothing for conditions to discriminate;
(b) the repair agent in E5/E6/E13 is the 1.5B model because both the 7B and
3B agents saturated the original benchmark (pass@1 $=100\%$)---with
failing-test feedback in the state, these archetypes are easy for capable
models. The capability ladder (1.5B: 70\%, 3B and 7B: 100\% on the original
suite) is itself a finding. All LLM-dependent numbers are
specific to the stated Ollama snapshots and Q4\_K\_M quantization.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}
We benchmark the substrate as a world computer on the thesis's three axes. \emph{Cost and
latency} to prototype were established in \S\ref{sec:proto-latency} (\ProtoNumWorlds{} Claude-Code-authored, conformant
worlds at a median of \ProtoMedianMinutes{} minutes, on consumer hardware). This section reports
\emph{performance}---rollout fidelity and runtime latency (\S\ref{sec:perf})---and the
\emph{evaluation and trust} machinery behind it: what each verification gate buys
(\S\ref{sec:guardrails}), judge-based selection (\S\ref{sec:judge-results}), composition
defeating the complexity cliff (\S\ref{sec:composition}), and the perceive$\to$world$\to$emit
boundary adding zero dynamics error (\S\ref{sec:perception}).

\subsection{Performance: rollout fidelity and runtime latency (E04, E11)}\label{sec:perf}
The two runtime properties a world computer must deliver are \emph{fidelity} (correct dynamics)
and \emph{latency} (fast dynamics). \textbf{Fidelity:} across the three oracle worlds, accepted
7B-synthesized dynamics are exact on \MultiCodeExact{} \RolloutSteps-step closed-loop rollouts
(95\% CI \MultiCodeCI) and answer $10\times$ out-of-distribution probes at \CodeOODRate{}, with no
compounding error---a single verified transition is reused at every step, so a depth-$t$ rollout
stays bit-exact rather than accumulating per-step error. We measure rule-level exactness only on
the three instrumented oracle worlds; for the \ProtoNumWorlds{} prototyped worlds we measure
conformance and executability, not fidelity to intent (\S\ref{sec:proto-latency}). \textbf{Latency:}
the same dynamics, as plain code, roll out at \CodeStepsPerSec{} steps/s, a $\sim$47{,}000$\times$
gap over the \LLMStepsPerSec{} steps/s of a \emph{per-step LLM} next-state proxy (E04). Two honest
caveats keep this in proportion: the comparison is ``compiled code vs.\ an LLM call every step,''
\emph{not} against a trained-then-compiled surrogate (which would be far faster than the LLM proxy,
though still learned and error-prone), and the LLM arm is a small timing sample; and the
hand-written function oracle is itself $\sim$16$\times$ faster than the synthesized code (the price
of synthesis generality). The point is not the headline multiple but that a verified world, once
built, runs at native code speed---fast and exact enough that rollout latency is never the
bottleneck, with the one-time synthesis cost (a few seconds, \S\ref{sec:proto-latency}) amortized
over unlimited rollouts (Figure~\ref{fig:worldcomputer}B).

\subsection{Verification guardrails, measured}\label{sec:guardrails}
\subsubsection{Verification-gate ablation: correctness gained per gate}\label{sec:abl1}
E3 ablates the acceptance regime over eight paired generations per condition,
using the 3B generator at high temperature so that defective candidates
actually occur (Figure~\ref{fig:verifier}). Blind acceptance of the first
code block yields mean ground-truth probe accuracy \VerifierNoneAcc;
syntax-only checking adds nothing (\VerifierNoneAcc---this generator's
failures parse fine); the full behavioral gate (sandboxed smoke-runs +
invariants + repair loop) lifts accuracy to \VerifierFullAcc, and adding the
7B semantic critic reaches \VerifierCriticAcc---a $+0.24$ absolute gain over
blind acceptance. Notably, no 3B generation achieved bit-exact dynamics under
any regime (the 7B generator routinely does): verification
buys a large, measurable correctness margin, but cannot substitute for
generator capability. Stated as the gate's \emph{false-accept rate} on branch
behavior---the quantity the single-initial-state smoke-run cannot see---every
one of the 3B generator's gate-accepted programs is wrong on at least one
branch-covering probe (a 100\% branch-level false-accept rate on this weak
generator), versus the 7B generator's routinely rule-perfect output. The
residual gap is semantic---code that runs and respects invariants can still
misread a rule---which is the gap the probe-and-tighten workflow
(\S\ref{sec:discussion}) targets, and which a branch-covering acceptance gate
(rather than a single-state smoke-run) is the natural next step to close.

\subsubsection{Closing the false-accept gap: a branch-covering gate (E62)}\label{sec:branch-gate}
The behavioral gate's blind spot is that it smoke-runs each action once, from the
initial state; a transition can be invariant-clean there yet violate an invariant
on a state reached only later in a rollout. E62 measures and closes this
deterministically (no LLM). On a panel of \BranchFaults{} faults that are clean from
the initial state but go negative on a reachable state (an unguarded \texttt{ship},
two off-by-one guards), the single-state gate's branch-level false-accept rate is
\BranchSingleFA\%---it accepts every one. Re-running each action over just
\BranchProbes{} branch-covering states (empty backlog, one outstanding bug, one unit
of debt) drops the false-accept rate to \BranchCovFA\%, while the correct program
still passes. With only \BranchFaults{} hand-built faults (as with the eight paired generations
in E3) this is a \emph{mechanism demonstration}, not a calibrated rate---a generated mutation
panel of dozens of faults would turn it into one, and is future work. The honest limit remains: a \emph{silent} correctness error
(invariant-clean everywhere but numerically wrong) passes \emph{both} gates---no
oracle-free gate can catch it, which is why we report exactness against ground-truth
probes, not acceptance, as the measure of correctness. The branch-covering gate is
now an option on the verifier (\texttt{probe\_states}); making branch coverage the
default acceptance criterion is the natural strengthening these numbers motivate.
E2 attempts compilation five times per world per model; E16 replicates the
protocol with generators from two other vendors
(Table~\ref{tab:synthesis-app}, Appendix~\ref{app:tables}). Every backbone
reaches 100\% verified acceptance within four iterations, but semantic
quality varies: accepted 7B Qwen code matches ground-truth probes at
\SynthProbeBig{} versus 0.87 for the 3B generator, while Llama-3.1-8B reaches
\LlamaProbe{} and Gemma-2-9B is \emph{rule-perfect on every attempt}
(\GemmaProbe), at mean synthesis costs of \LlamaWall\,s and \GemmaWall\,s per
world. The paradigm is not a Qwen artifact: three model families synthesize
verified dynamics in seconds on one laptop. Verification guarantees
executable, invariant-respecting code---not rule-perfect code; generator
capability still buys semantic fidelity.

E18 decomposes \emph{what verification does}
(Table~\ref{tab:repair-app}): with the same full gate, accepting only
first-shot candidates (no repair loop) accepts \FilterAccept{} of runs at
probe accuracy \FilterProbeAccepted{} among accepted, while enabling the
feedback loop lifts acceptance to \LoopAccept{} at \LoopProbeAccepted.
The filter is what protects quality; the repair loop is what recovers
throughput---it converts rejected generators into accepted ones without
degrading what gets through.

\subsection{Agents-as-a-judge}\label{sec:judge-results}
E13/E17 form the controlled comparison (Figure~\ref{fig:judge},
Table~\ref{tab:judge-controls}): for each paired round, the 1.5B proposer
samples three candidate patches \emph{once}, and four selection strategies
act on the same set. E13's 60 rounds put the judge at \CtrlJudge{} versus
\CtrlRandom{} for random selection (McNemar $p=\McNemarP$); the round-2
review asked for power, so E17 tripled the proposer seeds. Pooled over
\PooledRounds{} rounds (\NumTasks{} tasks $\times$ 6 seeds): first candidate
\PooledFirst, random \PooledRandom, judge \PooledJudge, oracle ceiling
\PooledOracle. The judge-vs-random margin replicated in direction and
sharpened---\PooledMcNemarB{} versus \PooledMcNemarC{} discordant rounds,
exact McNemar $p=\PooledMcNemarP$---but remains short of the conventional
threshold; the judge recovers roughly half of the random-to-oracle gap, and
we report the effect as estimated, not established. In full honesty, the
\NewRounds{} \emph{newly collected} rounds taken alone (judge \NewJudge{} vs
random \NewRandom) are not significant on their own (exact McNemar
$p=\NewMcNemarP$); the pooled $p=\PooledMcNemarP$ is reached only by combining
them with E13's original rounds, so it should be read as a pooled estimate
following an underpowered first result, not a pre-registered confirmatory test.
The position-bias audit
is the sharper finding: asked again with candidates in reversed order, the
judge's raw choices match on only \PooledConsistency{} of pooled rounds---yet
its \emph{accuracy} is order-stable, picking a passing candidate
\PooledFwdAcc{} of the time forward and \PooledRevAcc{} reversed on
discriminative rounds. Most inconsistency falls on rounds where several
candidates pass and the choice is inconsequential; the bias is real, but it
costs correctness little here. Cost accounting: judge-of-3 spends four model
calls per round (three 1.5B generations plus one 7B judgment) against one for
the single-proposal baseline, so the \PooledJudge-vs-\PooledFirst{} margin is
bought at roughly $4\times$ inference cost---worth it when test execution is
expensive or attempts are budgeted, not when proposals are free.

In the multi-attempt protocol (E5/E6), a single low-temperature 1.5B proposal
solves \BaselinePassOne{} of tasks first-try, and feedback-driven retries
recover nothing (pass@4 \BaselinePassBudget): the small agent resubmits
near-identical wrong patches, a local minimum. Judge-selected
high-temperature candidates trade first-attempt accuracy (\JudgePassOne{}
pass@1) for budgeted recovery (\JudgePassBudget{} pass@4)---diverse sampling
plus judgment escapes minima that deterministic retries cannot, at the cost
of noisier first shots. E7 and E15 close the loop on judge-as-objective:
across twelve dial-swept triage episodes, judge rubric scores track the
ground-truth aggregate at Spearman $\rho=\JudgeSpearman$ (permutation
$p=\RubricOrigP$); a paraphrased rubric is directionally consistent but
weaker and only marginal ($\rho=\RubricParaRho$, $p=\RubricParaP$)---rubric
wording matters. Together: judges are useful selectors and plausible
objectives, but their verdicts should gate, not replace, programmatic
verification.

\subsection{End-to-end evaluation}\label{sec:endtoend}
The coding world is itself the end-to-end test: world declaration, bit-exact
test-execution dynamics, an LLM agent in the loop, feedback-driven repair,
and judge-based selection compose into a system that repairs
\JudgePassBudget{} of benchmark defects with 1.5B/7B local models and no
training (and 100\% with a 7B agent alone)---every component of the
framework exercised against a verifiable external standard.

\subsection{Composition defeats the complexity cliff (E30--E32)}\label{sec:composition}
Figure~\ref{fig:composition} shows the anatomy under test: a composite world
is itself a world, its children run unmodified, and every coupling channel
is an explicit, independently verifiable object---bridges sideways, derived
aggregators upward, bindings downward, routes for agents.

\begin{figure}[htb]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{figs/composition.png}
\caption{\textbf{Anatomy of a composite world} (the E31 system, real state
values). Two country worlds, each containing two unmodified city worlds,
compose into a region. The four coupling channels are explicit objects: a
verified \emph{bridge} carries trade under a conservation invariant; a
dashed \emph{route} lets an agent cross (paying a toll into the destination
treasury); dashed upward arrows are \emph{aggregators}---parent summaries
derived from leaves, never independently simulated; the solid downward
arrow is a \emph{binding} injecting a parent parameter into a child.}
\label{fig:composition}
\end{figure}

E20 (the complexity cliff; companion paper~\cite{openworld_wtc}) left monolithic
synthesis collapsing past $R{\approx}8$ coupled rules and named composition
as the structural fix; E30 delivers it (Figure~\ref{fig:compcliff},
Table~\ref{tab:composition}). The
\emph{same} \CompRules-rule system that 7B monolithic synthesis renders at
\CompMonoAcc{} probe accuracy (95\% CI \CompMonoCI) reaches \CompCompAcc{}
(CI \CompCompCI) when posed as four \CompChildRules-rule children plus
verified bridges---every synthesized piece below the E20 cliff. Acceptance is
identical in the two conditions (every attempt passed the gate), so the
verifier is not the difference; the decomposition is. This is the
one-expert-per-piece structure PoE-World argues for~\cite{poeworld2025},
with each piece here passing the same verification pipeline as a whole world.

\begin{figure}[htb]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.72\linewidth]{figs/composition_cliff.png}
\caption{\textbf{Composition defeats the cliff.} The E20 curve (grey, with
pooled 95\% CI band): monolithic 7B synthesis accuracy falls as declared
interacting rules grow. At $R{=}16$, E30's monolithic condition (red) lands
on the fallen curve, while the same sixteen rules synthesized as four
4-rule children plus verified bridges (teal star) return to
near-$R{=}4$ accuracy.}
\label{fig:compcliff}
\end{figure}

The composition machinery itself adds no error. In E31, a three-level
composite (region $\to$ country $\to$ city) with a goods bridge, chained
aggregators, and a toll route is replayed against an independent flat-dict
oracle sharing no code with the framework: all \NestSteps{} steps are exact
(\NestExact{} on leaf exactness, aggregate consistency, money conservation,
and agent-registry agreement, including one paid and one vetoed crossing).
This holds essentially by construction---children run unmodified, aggregates
are derived rather than simulated---but the independent oracle makes it
evidence rather than assertion. The same hierarchy also defines what an
agent embedded in it can see (Figure~\ref{fig:traversal}): full detail at
its own node, derived aggregates of every ancestor, summaries of
route-adjacent neighbors, and nothing else---scoped observation that lets
planners reason locally against exact dynamics without ever holding the
macrostate.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{figs/traversal.png}
\caption{\textbf{What an agent sees} (E31, the trader at \texttt{c0:b}
after step 1). Its own city is fully observable; ancestor levels expose

The three mechanisms interact: rules can change \emph{inside} a composition and embodied
agents respond by moving. E33 (Figure~\ref{fig:dynamic}) demonstrates the loop
deterministically---when a shared economy enters austerity via \texttt{PhasedTransition} at
step \DynSwitchStep{} and the \emph{observed} rate collapses, a greedy agent prices the change
from its next observation and pays the \DynToll-coin toll to relocate, turning a regime shock
that would flatline GDP into a $+$\DynMobilityGain{} mobility gain. Composition supplies the
destination, dynamic rules the reason, and traversal the response.

E32 deserves a careful reading. Both phased and monolithic synthesized code
track the regime switch exactly (\RegimePhasedExact{} and \RegimeMonoExact{}
exact full rollouts; pre-boundary, boundary, and post-boundary accuracy all
perfect): a single declared rule change does not by itself defeat a 7B
generator at this scale. The LLM next-state proxy collapses on the same
script (\RegimeLLMPre{} pre-boundary, \RegimeLLMPost{} after the switch).
\texttt{PhasedTransition}'s value is therefore structural rather than a
fidelity rescue: each phase is synthesized and verified independently
against its own rule text, so changing rules \emph{compose} with the E30
result---adding a regime adds a phase, instead of multiplying the
complexity of a single synthesis prompt.

Finally, E34 puts the composite to work on the benchmark itself---a \emph{sprint world} whose
twenty children are the OpenWorld-SWE-bench repair worlds, with an allocation policy spending
\SprintBudget{} repair attempts (Figure~\ref{fig:sprint}). No policy beats the fixed protocol's
ceiling (\SprintFixedSolved{} solved, the first model-ladder numbers for the expanded
20-instance suite): round-robin matches it in roughly a third fewer attempts, while a greedy
closest-to-passing policy tarpits on a single capability-bound task (\SprintGreedySolved), a
pathology that persists even with per-attempt seeds restoring retry diversity. The composite's
value is efficiency and observability, not a higher ceiling---allocation cannot buy what the
model cannot produce.

\subsection{Feeding the real world in: multimodal perception (E39--E40)}\label{sec:perception}
The worlds so far are driven by symbolic inputs. A \emph{perception boundary}
extends them to text, audio, and video without touching the verified core: an
untrusted \texttt{Perceptor} maps raw input to a partial symbolic state update,
a gate contract-checks it (owned fields, types, ranges), and only then does it
reach the dynamics---before any transition runs. Raw media never enters state
(only the resolved symbolic delta and a content hash), so determinism and
bit-exact replay are preserved; perception is the one acknowledged fallible,
non-deterministic layer, and is \emph{measured}, never reported as verified.

This is what lets messy real-world observations drive an exact model of a
real-world phenomenon. E40 takes a discrete SIR epidemic---the textbook case
where the decision that matters is a multi-step \emph{forecast}, not a one-step
read---and drives it from perceived day-0 inputs (a text situation report and a
ward-photo frame, resolved through the boundary into $S,I,R$), then rolls the
verified dynamics forward \PercHorizon{} days. The perceived world model
forecasts the day-\PercHorizon{} infected count \emph{exactly}, both
in-distribution (\PercWorldIn) and at $10\times$ population scale
(\PercWorldOod), where an end-to-end MLP given the same inputs scores
\PercMlpIn{} in-distribution (\PercMlpInTol{} within $\pm2$) and \PercMlpOod{}
out of distribution (Figure~\ref{fig:perception}A), while rolling the declared
model forward is exact at any scale. Crucially, the value survives
imperfect perception in a principled way. Because the dynamics add zero error,
end-to-end forecast accuracy equals \emph{perception} accuracy
(Figure~\ref{fig:perception}B): a perceptor right 75\% of the time yields 75\%
correct forecasts, no worse. E39 confirms this decomposition holds exactly
across perception error rates $0$--$0.9$ (\PercDecompHolds). The practical
reading: a symbolic world model fed by perception models a real phenomenon as
well as its declared rules and its perception allow---and the architecture
keeps those two error sources separate and individually measurable, with the
dynamics contributing none.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}\label{sec:discussion}
\paragraph{Interpretation.} The experiments quantify a structural trade.
Learned world models buy pixel-native breadth with training compute and pay
in compounding error, opacity, and frozen values. Verified code synthesis
buys exactness, speed, OOD transfer, and auditability, and pays at the
boundary of what can be declared symbolically. Within that boundary the
results are one-sided: a laptop-scale local model, orchestrated as
plan--generate--verify, produces simulators that are \emph{perfect} where
learned-style baselines are approximate. The practical workflow the ablations
license: declare rules precisely; verify with invariants; probe the accepted
artifact against intent; tighten and recompile. Each gate buys measurable
correctness, and the artifact remains a diffable program at every stage.

\paragraph{What these worlds enable: world-time compute.} Beyond serving as simulators to plan \emph{in}, verified worlds are also training \emph{signal}: because each world labels trajectories exactly for free, fine-tuning on trajectories from many worlds of a domain lifts an LLM's generalization to held-out worlds (\emph{world-time compute}). That result---its predictive regularity, its boundaries, and how it relates to self-training---is the subject of the companion paper~\cite{openworld_wtc}; here we only note that the framework's exact, composable worlds are what make it possible.

\paragraph{Value alignment as an open specification.} The dial experiments
ground the alignment claim: because objectives are declared artifacts, the
welfare--fairness frontier is \emph{operational}---an operator moves along it
in real time, and a tuner searches moral configurations jointly with world
design, surfacing the manifold of value settings that solve a task rather
than a single frozen compromise. Two qualifications, owed to the
philosophical review. First, openness is only as wide as the expressible
structures: a weighted sum is act consequentialism, and
\S\ref{sec:moral-structure} shows that aggregator choice, side constraints,
and theory uncertainty are distinct moral objects the framework must (and
now does) represent rather than approximate with weights. Second, open
specification \emph{manages} the specification trap~\cite{spectrap2025}
procedurally rather than resolving it: the trap relocates from frozen
weights to the operator's hand on the dial, and which hand that should
be---justifiability to those affected, in the contractualist's
sense~\cite{scanlon1998}---is a question no architecture settles. The
tuner's solving manifold is at best a crude contractualist surface: the set
of configurations meeting every stakeholder's declared minimum.

\paragraph{Limitations.} (i) Scope: symbolic-state worlds; pixel-native
domains remain the territory of learned models---the comparison here is
within the symbolic regime, and neither the LLM proxy nor the numpy
baselines is a trained Dreamer. The headline comparison also gives the
synthesizer the rule text; E37 shows the structural OOD advantage survives
removing that asymmetry (induction from traces alone), but induction is then
imperfect (\IndTracesIn{} vs the rule-text \IndRulesIn), so part of the
headline margin reflects the value of an explicit specification, not the
representation alone. (ii) Complexity: monolithic synthesis with a
7B generator is reliable at four interacting rules and unreliable past
roughly eight (E20); the headline worlds sit below that cliff, and richer
worlds will need compositional synthesis~\cite{poeworld2025} or larger
generators. (iii) Scale: twenty repair tasks and three handwritten worlds;
paired tests sharpen the comparisons, but the benchmark remains an archetype
suite, not HumanEval~\cite{humaneval2021}, 3B+ agents saturate it, and the
classic defect archetypes are plausibly present in pretraining data: agent
solve rates should not be read as measurements of debugging skill---the
world-model claims are unaffected, since the environment executes exactly
regardless of what the agent has memorized. (iv) Synthesis replicates across
three model families (E16), but the judge, critic, and repair-agent roles
remain Qwen-only, and all experiments ran on one machine under the stated
quantizations. (v) Judges: selection accuracy and order-consistency are
measured but imperfect, the pooled selection margin is marginal
($p=\PooledMcNemarP$) and costs $\sim$4$\times$ inference, and rubric grading
showed compression; judge verdicts should gate, not replace, programmatic
verification. (vi) Ensemble self-checking (E23) achieved perfect
precision/recall here but is structurally vulnerable to correlated errors
across generators reading the same ambiguous rule; it is a screen, not a
proof. (vii) The sandbox is best-effort isolation against accident, not an
adversarial security boundary (generated patches twice defeated in-process
timeouts before the fork-based runner; see the repository history).
(viii) The moral machinery, even broadened, evaluates declared values; whose
values are declared---and the legitimacy of the declaring hand---is a
procedural question outside any architecture~\cite{scanlon1998}, and the
parliament's credences are themselves operator-set dials. (ix) Several
experiments were redesigned after pilots and four internal review rounds
(E3, E5/E6, E11--E27); pilot saturation results are reported alongside the
final protocols, and all reviews ship in the repository.
(x) Multiple comparisons: across \NumExperiments{} experiments we report many
$p$-values and rank correlations and apply \emph{no} family-wise or
false-discovery correction; with this many tests the borderline results
($p$ between $0.05$ and $0.10$---notably the judge margin and the
rubric-paraphrase correlation) should be read as exploratory, and only the
large deterministic effects (exact-match rates, OOD collapse, the complexity
cliff) are robust to the multiplicity. (xi) Variance: the deterministic claims
are exact-match against oracles and need no error bars, but several
\emph{stochastic} (LLM) headline quantities are point estimates from small,
single-seed samples (e.g.\ the E1 divergence step, $n{=}3$, one world) without
across-seed variance; we add the pooled multi-world view (E11) where it
matters, but per-model probe accuracies should be read as estimates, not tight
measurements. (xii) Two illustrative studies are true \emph{by construction}:
E59's architecture lift follows arithmetically from a modeled fixed-capability
backbone, and E60's routing gap over exact-key lookup is guaranteed by the
comparison design (we add a fair lexical baseline and report the modest gap over
it); both are flagged in situ as engineering demonstrations, not measured effects.
(xiii) Porting to real data surfaces two validity conditions: the input representation must
encode \emph{per-world} structure (a ProteinGym port describing each mutation only by global
biochemical deltas made every assay-world identical and flattened the world-count curve until
per-world local-sequence context was restored), and faithful data loading---e.g.\ the wild-type
sequence and one-based mutation indexing---is the dominant correctness risk. We treat both as the
first checks any real-domain port must pass.
(xiv) Adoption gaps, stated plainly. The \texttt{serve} layer is a \emph{stateless local
inference endpoint} for trusted specs, not a hardened multi-tenant service: it has no auth, rate
limiting, or persistence, and \texttt{allow\_code=True} executes synthesized code in the
best-effort (non-adversarial) sandbox---hosting third-party specs would need a real security
boundary. (xv) Debugging a \emph{silently wrong} world (accepted but rule-incorrect) currently
relies on probe-and-tighten, which presupposes ground-truth probes the author of a novel world
lacks; automatic probe generation (generalizing the branch-covering panel) and differential
testing against an independent second synthesis are the natural tools, and are future work.
(xvi) The portable spec carries a version, but we make no cross-version migration guarantee yet;
published specs may need updating as the format evolves past \texttt{1.0}.

\section{Conclusion}
A training-free symbolic world model---synthesized as code by a local model and verified
before acceptance---is bit-exact where learned-style dynamics compound error, and plans
\emph{as well as the ground truth itself} where planning through a hallucinating model is
worse than no model. And beyond serving as a simulator to plan \emph{in}, the same
verified worlds can serve as training \emph{signal}---fine-tuning on trajectories from many of
them lifts generalization to held-out worlds---which the companion paper~\cite{openworld_wtc}
develops as \emph{world-time compute}. Its boundary is equally measured: monolithic 7B
synthesis fails past roughly eight interacting rules. The world-model field's
trajectory has been to scale parameters until hallucination is suppressed;
these results argue for an orthogonal path---make the model a program, verify
the program, and keep the values dialable. Next steps: compositional
synthesis to cross the complexity cliff~\cite{poeworld2025}, the coding world
at standard-benchmark scale, hybrid perception-to-symbol pipelines, and
judges that propose---not just select---world repairs.

\section{The synthesized dynamics artifact}\label{app:figs}
A representative accepted sprint-world transition, synthesized by
\texttt{qwen2.5:7b} and verified by the full gate---the artifact whose
exactness underwrites the head-to-head results (companion paper~\cite{openworld_wtc}):

\begin{tcolorbox}[policycard, cardcol=cardteal,
  title=Accepted \texttt{transition()} (verbatim)]
\begin{lstlisting}[style=housespecyamlbare]
def transition(state, action):
    next_state = dict(state)
    name = action["name"]
    if name == "ship" and next_state["backlog"] > 0:
        next_state["backlog"] -= 1
        next_state["shipped"] += 1
        next_state["debt"] += 1
        next_state["bugs"] += next_state["debt"] // 4
    elif name == "fix":
        next_state["bugs"] = max(0, next_state["bugs"] - 2)
    elif name == "refactor":
        next_state["debt"] = max(0, next_state["debt"] - 2)
    return next_state
\end{lstlisting}
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Example tasks and failure cases}\label{app:examples}
The coding-world tasks pair a buggy function with hidden tests; e.g.\
\texttt{dedupe} returns \texttt{list(set(xs))} (order-destroying) and must
preserve first-seen order, and \texttt{balanced} counts parenthesis depth but

attempt budget expired on tasks where its first reading of the spec was

\subsection{Portable world-model specs and model cards (E57)}\label{sec:specs}
For world models to be shared the way trained models are, a world needs a
\emph{portable, complete, lossless} serialization --- the unit a ``marketplace
for world models'' would host. Sharing matters wherever a verified simulator
outlives the session that built it: reusing an audited world across teams or
agents, composing a third party's verified world into one's own, distributing a
planning environment as a benchmark, or shipping a model card a consumer can
check before trusting what a world claims. We define a JSON spec that captures every
component of a world model (Figure~\ref{fig:world-specs}B): its name and
metadata, state schema and concrete initial state, action set, the
natural-language \emph{rules} (the declared contract), the verified dynamics (as
executable code, recursively for composites), the \emph{perception} boundary
(perceptors: modality, the state fields they produce, and a typed schema), the
symmetric \emph{emit} boundary, objectives, and evaluation metrics. Composition
is recursive, so a world-of-worlds (\S\ref{sec:composition}) serializes whole.

The spec is lossless: \texttt{from\_spec(to\_spec(w),\,allow\_code=True)}
reconstructs a runnable world whose rollout reproduces the original exactly. On
\SpecsNumWorlds{} worlds spanning leaves and a two-level nested composite, all
round-trip exactly and pass the publish-gate validator
(Table~\ref{tab:worldspecs}, Figure~\ref{fig:world-specs}A). For untrusted
downloads the embedded code is inert unless execution is explicitly opted into ---
a trust gate, not a sandbox. From a spec the framework renders a self-contained
SVG \emph{model card} (Figure~\ref{fig:world-card}): a leaf world is drawn as its
actual state-transition automaton (computed by BFS from the initial state) with
the perceive$\to$world$\to$emit boundary shown explicitly, and a composite as its
recursively nested dataflow of child worlds, bridges, and aggregator roll-ups.
The same spec also exports to Mermaid and to React Flow (a node/edge graph with
nested parent groups) for interactive use, and a gallery contact sheet indexes a
collection. The contribution is not a new result but an \emph{interface}: the
paper's world models become publishable, inspectable, verifiable artifacts.

\subsection{Steerability and the structure of the moral filter (E8, E24--E27)}\label{app:moral-dial}
Objectives and dials are a secondary contribution---they make values an editable,
inference-time artifact---but they raise questions a world-models paper need not
settle in its main text, so the steerability result and its moral-philosophy
critique are collected here.

\paragraph{Steerability: the moral dial.}\label{sec:robustness}
E8 sweeps the moral weight $\lambda \in [0,1]$ on the commons world
(Figure~\ref{fig:pareto}). All \ParetoFrontierSize{} of \ParetoLambdas{}
sweep points are Pareto-optimal, fairness is monotone in $\lambda$, and the
Nash bargaining point sits strictly interior at $\lambda=\NashLambda$---the
``interior optimum'' behavior predicted by the tunable-morality literature,
reproduced here with a dial that can be turned mid-simulation rather than a
retrained model~\cite{spectrap2025,mas2025,stablevalue2026}.

\paragraph{The structure of the moral filter.}\label{sec:moral-structure}
A fourth review round, written from a moral philosopher's standpoint
(\texttt{docs/review/}), pressed a charge the engineering rounds could not:
the dial system is not a neutral container for ethics. Everything
expressible as \texttt{Objective}+\texttt{Dial} reduces to
$\sum_i w_i o_i$---act consequentialism with a linear social welfare
function---so the ``morality dial'' selects points in one theory's parameter
space, not among theories. Four experiments, enabled by a new
\texttt{openworld.ethics} module (Rawlsian and lexicographic aggregators,
deontological constraints as action vetoes, and a moral parliament),
measure what that restriction costs and what lifting it buys.

\paragraph{Aggregation is an ethical commitment (E24).} On a scarce commons
where one agent harvests faster than the other, the utilitarian sum,
Rawlsian maximin~\cite{rawls1971}, and leximin choose different operating
points from the same outcome table: the sum is \emph{indifferent} between
harvests of $(6,3)$ and $(5,4)$---both total 9---and so picks the unequal
allocation arbitrarily, while maximin and leximin select the restraint level
that lifts the worst-off from \AggUtilWorst{} to \AggMaximinWorst{} at zero
total cost. Distribution-blindness is not a hypothetical defect of the
weighted sum; it selects a different world even when an egalitarian free
lunch exists.

\paragraph{Side constraints cannot be simulated by weights (E25).} A myopic
cost-weighted nurse becomes \emph{impermissible} at $\lambda > 1$: the
weighting makes abandoning waiting critical patients optimal
(\ViolationsDialOnly{} violations across the sweep, four deteriorations,
outcomes collapsing to $-8$). A Nozickian side constraint~\cite{nozick1974}
---``never choose another action while a critical patient waits,'' enforced
as a veto on the action set, not a penalty in the score---eliminates every
violation at every $\lambda$, and here costs nothing in the dial's
permissible region (\ConstraintOutcomeCost{} outcome points) while
\emph{rescuing} the impermissible one. Penalties are just more
utilitarianism; vetoes are a different kind of object, and the framework now
has both.

\paragraph{Moral uncertainty and the parliament (E26).} An operator who
does not know the right theory should hedge rather than maximize within one
theory~\cite{macaskill2020}. Delegates for utilitarian, Rawlsian, and
deontological positions vote (credence-weighted Borda) over each action; on
the triage shift, the parliament is never the strictly worst agent under
\emph{any} theory's own lens---and notably it rescues the lone Rawlsian
delegate from itself: pure one-step maximin lets critical patients
deteriorate (worst-off welfare $-6$, two duty violations), while the
parliament's mixed counsel reaches the same outcome as the best
single-theory agents ($-3$, zero violations).

\paragraph{Pluralism is visible in the judge (E27).} Grading the same
twelve dial-swept trajectories under utilitarian, deontological, and
care-ethics rubrics yields orderings that correlate only partially
(pairwise Spearman \RubricPluralismRange; the deontological and care
rubrics track the dial differently than the utilitarian one). Rubric
framing is a value choice, not a wording choice---a quantitative
counterpart to Berlin's incommensurability claim~\cite{berlin1969} and a
caution for anyone deploying a single ``ethics rubric'' judge.

\subsection{An emergent economy from composed verified rules (E44)}\label{sec:economy}
This study scales composition from belief-tracking to a small open
world: \EconAgents{} agents that gather, sell, and accumulate gold over
\EconTicks{} ticks, assembled entirely from separable verified rules and the
framework's \texttt{CompositeWorld}/\texttt{Aggregator} machinery. Each agent is
a real child world whose tick produces goods (with a concave capital term, so
wealth begets output at diminishing returns); the macro quantities we report---
money supply, market price, and the wealth Gini---are \emph{aggregators} derived
from the leaves, so a headline number can never drift from the micro state. The
market price, the gold faucet (sale revenue), the burn-tax sink, and
redistribution are each their own toggleable rule. Two properties make this more
than a simulator demo. First, the macro phenomena \emph{emerge}: nothing in the
rules targets a price level, an inflation rate, or an inequality coefficient.
Second---because every rule is a separate verified component---we read each
phenomenon's cause as a clean counterfactual by switching one rule on or off,
which a black-box economy cannot offer.

Four phenomena emerge and each yields to its causal toggle
(Figure~\ref{fig:economy}). \textbf{Price formation:} the market price converges
to a level set by supply and demand, not scripted---scarcer supply clears at a
higher price (\EconScarcePrice{} vs.\ \EconAbundantPrice{} when supply doubles).
\textbf{Inflation:} with the burn sink off, gold only enters and the money
supply chases a fixed stock of goods, inflating price at slope
\EconInflOffSlope{}/tick; turning the verified tax sink on bends that to
\EconInflOnSlope{}---inflation isolated to one rule. \textbf{Inequality:} under
selfish accumulation the Gini climbs to \EconGiniOff{} as capital compounds;
enabling the redistribution rule collapses it to \EconGiniOn{}. \textbf{The
selfish--cooperative dial:} flipping agents from individual gold-maximization to
welfare-sharing raises \emph{total} welfare by \EconCoopWelfareGain\% (\EconSelfishWelfare{}
$\to$ \EconCoopWelfare{} gold)---because concave capital means an equal spread
produces more---yet the single greediest selfish agent still ends richer than
any cooperator (\EconSelfishMax{} vs.\ \EconCoopMax{} gold). This is the E08
welfare--fairness Pareto tension of the moral dial (\S\ref{sec:robustness}),
now \emph{emerging} from a composed verified economy rather than declared as a
scalar reward, and every tick the books balance exactly: total gold moves only
by the faucet inflow minus the sink outflow, asserted throughout the run.

\subsection{Finding generalizing world-combinations by search (E80)}\label{sec:combo-search}
The \ProtoNumWorlds{} prototyped worlds (E68) are deliberately heterogeneous and globally do not
transfer (E71): a policy learned on one is no better than random on another. But heterogeneity is
a property of the \emph{set}, not of every \emph{subset}. So we ask a combinatorial question---is
there a \emph{combination} of worlds that generalizes, and can the coherence proxy \emph{find}
it? Brute force is hopeless ($2^{\ComboNWorlds}$ subsets over the \ComboNWorlds{} steerable
worlds), so we use world$\to$world transfer competence as a cheap coherence signal, grow subsets
greedily from the most-coherent seed pairs, and confirm each candidate by training a shared
abstract policy on the subset \emph{minus one world} and evaluating the held-out member (a strict
world-level holdout). Random subsets are the null control.

The search succeeds on its \emph{first} coherence-ranked seed: a \ComboSize{}-world subset
spanning \ComboNSectors{} different sectors (\texttt{\ComboWorlds}) generalizes at planner-level
held-out competence (\ComboHeldout, clipped at the planner ceiling) versus \ComboBaseline{} for
random subsets. The worlds are surface-unrelated---minimise risk, minimise cost, minimise a
waitlist, maximise margin---but share an \emph{abstract control structure}, and that shared skill
is what the proxy detects. Two consequences. First, ``domain'' is the wrong unit: generalization
tracks shared \emph{structure}, which can cross sectors. Second, the coherence proxy is not only
predictive but \emph{constructive}---it turns the NP-hard search for a generalizing world-family
into a guided one and locates a family inside a set built to have none, while the random-subset
control stays at the heterogeneous floor (the law's null, confirmed).

\subsection{Why frozen perceptual world models don't control cartpole (E67)}\label{sec:vjepa-control}
The cartpole head-to-head (companion paper~\cite{openworld_wtc}) gives the perceptual species its
best shot and it still scores \CartVjepaSolve{}. Because that is a strong claim, we
chased it down rather than assert it. Every way we tried to turn a frozen visual encoder
into a swing-up controller failed: (i)~planning over a learned latent-dynamics model on
frozen V-JEPA-2 features; (ii)~the same with a learned latent \emph{reward} head (so the
planning objective is no longer the weak link); (iii)~behavioral cloning of the OpenWorld
CEM expert; (iv)~DAgger, querying that expert on the policy's \emph{own} visited states
(curing covariate shift); and (v)~frame-stacked DAgger, giving the policy velocity. All
five score $0$.

A natural explanation is that the frozen features simply don't see the pole. They do. A
small probe recovers the \emph{full} state from frozen DINOv2 features---$R^2(\sin\theta)
{=}\CartDinoSinTheta$, $R^2(\cos\theta){=}\CartDinoCosTheta$, and comparably the cart
position and angular velocity---with ImageNet ViT close behind (Table~\ref{tab:cartpole_ablation}).
(An earlier probe that regressed the raw, \emph{periodic} angle reported $R^2{\approx}0$
and briefly fooled us; that is a metric artifact---$\theta$ and $\theta{+}2\pi$ render
identically---which $\sin/\cos$ decoding corrects.) So the representation is sufficient;
what fails is converting good \emph{frozen} features into stable control through any
bolt-on controller. DreamerV3 succeeds precisely because it trains its encoder end to end
\emph{for} prediction and control. The lesson is clean and, we think, transferable: strong
frozen perceptual features are necessary but not sufficient for control---end-to-end task
training, not perception, is what closes the gap.

\subsection{A database for many worlds: holding a non-enumerable version space (E46)}\label{sec:many-worlds}
E43 maintains its version space as an explicit list of candidate worlds, which
works for hundreds but cannot survive the regime where parameter counts and
ranges grow and the world space becomes effectively infinite. E46 supplies the
data structure that does. The transition of a parametric world family decomposes
into \emph{mechanisms}, each predicting one observable of the next state from the
observed state and a small \emph{scope} of parameters (for the sprint family:
the ship debt increment depends only on \texttt{ship\_debt}, the bug interaction
only on $k$, and so on). Because each transition is observed in full, the
likelihood factorises over observables, so the store keeps one small factor per
mechanism scope rather than the global product---a finite, symbolic
representation of an enormous world set, in the spirit of conditional-table and
provenance-semiring databases. The factor values live in a pluggable \emph{semiring} (an algebra with an
``add'' and a ``multiply'' operation), so the \emph{same} store answers Boolean (is a world still possible?), counting
(how many worlds remain?), and probability (a posterior over parameters)
queries; an observation reweights only the touched factors, and a prediction
marginalises only the relevant one.

The payoff is the version-space result scaled past enumeration
(Figure~\ref{fig:many-worlds}, Table~\ref{tab:many-worlds}). On small spaces the
factored store's count, marginals, and expected-next-state match brute-force
enumeration \emph{exactly}. On large ones it maintains an exact version space and
posterior over \ManyWorldsMax{} worlds in \ManyWorldsMaxMs{}\,ms---narrowing them
to \ManyWorldsConsistent{} consistent worlds and peaking the posterior on the
true rule---where the explicit enumeration of E43 is infeasible beyond
\ManyWorldsEnumMax{}. Update and query cost scale as the \emph{sum} of the
parameter-domain sizes rather than their product. The boundary is the
probabilistic-database one: when a mechanism couples $w$ parameters
non-separably its factor grows as $d^{w}$ (at $w{=}\ManyWorldsCoupleWidth$ the
factor holds \ManyWorldsCoupleFactor{} entries versus \ManyWorldsCoupleIdeal{}
for the separable ideal), degrading toward enumeration---the $\#$P-hardness of
exact query evaluation, surfaced and measured rather than hidden. Together with
E43 this is active inference over a world space too large to write down:
active induction (companion paper~\cite{openworld_wtc}) chooses actions to shrink the set, E46 holds and
queries it.

\subsection{Path integrals over learning trajectories (E49)}\label{sec:path-integral}
E46 searches the space of worlds for a problem; E49 asks how an agent should
\emph{traverse} that space---in what order to master worlds---to solve a new,
out-of-distribution problem as directly as possible. We cast it as a path
integral over learning trajectories: a trajectory is an ordering of skills to
learn, weighted by $e^{-\beta S}$ with the action $S$ the trajectory's total
learning cost---the sum of per-skill costs (in abstract effort units), each skill
cheap to acquire once its prerequisites are mastered and dear from scratch; the
sum is dominated by the least-action path, the most direct curriculum. Crucially
this needs \emph{no new machinery}: a path integral is a sum over paths, which is
a semiring computation, so we reuse the E46 \texttt{Semiring} and swap the value
ring---tropical $(\min,+)$ for the least-action path, log (log-sum-exp,~$+$) for
the full partition function---and run a forward/backward dynamic program (DP) over
capability-states. The combinatorially many trajectories are summed
\emph{without enumeration}: \PathTraj{} distinct trajectories are accounted for by
a DP over just \PathStates{} states.

Skills compose cheaply once their prerequisites are mastered and are expensive to
learn from scratch, and the target (``\PathGoal'') is a \emph{novel composition},
so the least-action path reuses mastered sub-worlds---compositional transfer
rather than learning the target blind. The agent's trajectory is built
\emph{from its spec}: a senior SWE, a director, and a CEO start with different
capabilities and get different optimal curricula to the same goal (cost
\PathSweCost{}, \PathDirCost{}, \PathCeoCost{} respectively;
Table~\ref{tab:path-integral}, Figure~\ref{fig:path-integral}). All three are
far below unplanned baselines---random ordering averages \PathRandom{} because it
pays the from-scratch penalty for learning composites before their
prerequisites---and far below learning the goal from scratch (\PathScratch{}), a
$\PathSpeedup\times$ compositional-transfer speedup. As the inverse temperature
$\beta$ grows the free energy $-\tfrac1\beta\log Z$ rises from the
entropy-dominated regime (many near-equivalent orderings) to the least-action
cost---the path integral collapsing onto the optimal curriculum---while the
forward$\times$backward marginals flag exactly the worlds that lie on it. The
bridge is the point: one semiring engine now answers version-space, counting,
posterior \emph{and} optimal-trajectory queries over composite-world spaces, and
turns an agent spec into a concrete learning path toward an OOD goal.

\subsection{Next-token world models: exact length generalization (E45)}\label{sec:next-token}
The induction results so far live in the framework's own state--action worlds.
E45 lands the same thesis on the LLM field's native task---next-token
prediction---and its native failure, length generalization on algorithmic
sequences. The framing is ``synthesize the rule, don't \emph{be} the rule'': the
\emph{same} local model (qwen3-coder:30b), given short solved examples, is used two
ways---asked to predict the next character directly, and asked to synthesise a
verified program (a world model of the sequence's generator) that is then run.
We use \NextTokTasks{} deterministic tasks whose next character needs memory a
fixed window cannot hold: \texttt{parity} (running XOR), \texttt{dyck}
(bracket-nesting depth), and \texttt{copy} (a long-range retrieval: the answer is
the first character of the context). Models are trained on lengths up to
\NextTokTrainLen{} and tested out to \NextTokMaxLen{}.

Where synthesis recovers the rule it does so exactly, and the induced program is
\emph{length-invariant by construction}: it is exact at every length on
\NextTokExactTasks/\NextTokTasks{} tasks (\NextTokSymOod{} mean out-of-distribution
accuracy), because it recomputes its state from the whole prefix rather than a
fixed window (Figure~\ref{fig:next-token}, Table~\ref{tab:next-token}). Every
learned alternative decays with length: the $n$-gram (\NextTokNgramOod{} OOD) and
the fixed-window MLP (\NextTokMlpOod{}) cannot represent unbounded-memory rules,
and the \emph{same} 30B predicting directly---good in-distribution
(\NextTokDirectIn)---falls to \NextTokDirectOod{} out of distribution: the
canonical transformer length-generalization failure, with the synthesise-a-program
mode of the identical model as the remedy.

This applies where the next-token process has exact structure---code, formal
languages, grammar- or JSON-constrained generation, tool and protocol
outputs---the regime where an agent can profitably call a synthesized model
rather than predict tokens directly.

\subsection{A composite corporate world: individual, division, and company goals (E48)}\label{sec:corporate}
The framework's structural primitives---nested composition, aggregation,
hierarchical observation, multi-level objectives---map almost one-to-one onto an
organization, so E48 models a DigitalOcean-style PaaS company
(\CorpDivisions{} divisions, \$\CorpRevenue{}M revenue) as a nested
\texttt{CompositeWorld}: company $\triangleright$ divisions $\triangleright$
individuals, with revenue rolled up by \texttt{Aggregator}s and agents (senior
SWE, director, CEO) acting at different scope (Figure~\ref{fig:corporate},
Table~\ref{tab:corporate}). Division revenue has diminishing returns in effort,
so the company-optimal allocation spreads effort by marginal return---and
individual-optimal diverges from it. Perception is supplied by
\textbf{openworld-corp}, a committed dataset of \CorpTranscripts{} LLM-generated,
state-grounded transcripts (all-hands, exec staff, division reviews, retros,
standups, 1:1s, Slack) across three quarters; it is meant to be \emph{used}---
swap the org seed or drop in a real company's transcripts (same schema) to run
the model on an actual organization.

Four agent-level results. \emph{Individual vs collective}: as a selfishness dial
turns agents from company-aligned to promotion-chasing, company growth falls from
\CorpAlignedGrowth\% to \CorpSelfishGrowth\% while the promotion Gini rises from
\CorpAlignedGini{} to \CorpSelfishGini---local optimization concentrates rewards
and starves the aggregate (the E44/E08 Pareto on an org chart). \emph{Value-of-
action}: optimizing each level's decision in turn, the CEO's portfolio
reallocation is the dominant lever ($+\CorpCeoPct\%$ company growth) over a
director's ($+\CorpDirPct\%$) and an IC's ($+\CorpIcPct\%$)---leverage lives at
the top. \emph{Perception is granularity-bound}: division-level metrics are
recoverable from both the all-hands and a division review (they are stated), but
the individual promotion signal is recovered \CorpIndivOneOnOne\% of the time
from a 1:1 and \CorpIndivAllHands\% from the all-hands---aggregate meetings
preserve division state and lose individual state, so an agent must traverse to
the right meeting for the right decision (the hierarchical-observation contract,
\S\ref{sec:traversal}). \emph{Navigation policy}: at every level a principled
(marginal-value) policy beats a greedy one. Throughout, the revenue aggregator
never drifts from the leaves---the composite stays exact.

\subsection{A startup growth world model: what drives a YC-style batch (E51)}\label{sec:startups}
The same composite machinery as the corporate world (E48), turned on a cohort of
startups: a batch of \StartupN{} startups is a \texttt{CompositeWorld} of startup
child-worlds, each evolving from its factors (team, market, product-market fit,
grit, capital). Growth is multiplicative in PMF (no fit, no growth); revenue
compounds; burn depletes runway; traction-gated fundraising extends it;
idiosyncratic shocks kill some regardless of merit; the batch's total value
created, survival rate, and top-decile value share are \texttt{Aggregator}s
(Figure~\ref{fig:startups}, Table~\ref{tab:startups}).

Four results. \emph{What drives growth (causal value-of-factor)}: lifting each
factor to its 90th percentile across the batch, PMF moves total value most
(\StartupPmfDelta\%), while doubling everyone's capital moves it
\StartupCapitalDelta\%---money is not the constraint, fit is. \emph{Power law of
returns}: only \StartupSurvival\% survive and the top decile captures
\StartupTopDecile\% of all value (Gini \StartupGini)---the venture home-run
dynamic. \emph{Counterfactual attribution}: a no-PMF batch produces
\StartupNoPmf\% of base value, and doubling capital while holding PMF low yields
\StartupCapLowPmf\%---spend cannot substitute for fit, separating the true driver
from the spend. \emph{Bounded predictability}: month-6 traction is informative but
not decisive (Spearman \StartupSpearman{}; only \StartupTopOverlap\% of the
eventual top decile were already in the month-6 top decile)---startup outcomes are
more predictable than market moves (E50), because they are driven by persistent
factors, yet the tail stays luck-laden, so the single biggest winner is hard to
call early. The composite stays exact: the batch value aggregator never drifts
from the leaves.

\subsection{A brain simulator: tree-of-thoughts ReAct with real memory (E58)}\label{sec:brain}
As a capstone for composition + perception + agency, we build a \emph{brain} as a
world-of-worlds (Figure~\ref{fig:brain-card}): a \textsc{conscious} world (working
memory --- the current thought and plan), an \textsc{unconscious} world (long-term,
content-addressable memory: a learned transition model and cached plans), and an
\textsc{environment} world the brain acts on. A perceptor maps an outside situation
into the conscious world; bridges \emph{retrieve} memories into working memory,
\emph{consolidate} experience into long-term memory, and \emph{act} on the
environment --- the perceive$\to$world$\to$act loop. The whole brain serializes to a
spec and round-trips losslessly (\S\ref{sec:specs}).

An agent runs a ReAct loop with \emph{tree-of-thoughts}: perceive $\to$ retrieve
$\to$ \emph{think} (expand a depth-$D$ tree over the learned model to find a plan to
the goal) $\to$ act on the real environment $\to$ observe $\to$ consolidate. Memory
is real and persistent, so a re-encountered situation is recalled, not re-derived.
On a deterministic $\BrainNodes$-node navigation environment (optimal path length
$L=\BrainOptimal$), two results hold (Figure~\ref{fig:brain}, Table~\ref{tab:brain}):
\textbf{(A) memory wins} --- the brain's persistent unconscious drives steps-to-goal
down to the optimal $L=\BrainFinal$ within a couple of episodes, while a memoryless
agent (unconscious wiped each episode) stays flat at $\sim\BrainNoMem$ and a random
agent flails at $\sim\BrainRandom$; \textbf{(B) how much to think} --- with the model
known, steps fall as the planning depth $D$ rises and \emph{plateau} once
$D\!\geq\!$ the needed horizon (here $D=\BrainPlateau$): bounded simulated lookahead
helps exactly up to the goal horizon, and no further. This is a controlled synthetic
cognitive task, not a claim about real brains --- it shows the framework can compose
a brain-like architecture and that memory and bounded lookahead measurably help.

\subsection{The brain around a frozen model, as a composable world (E59)}\label{sec:brainarch}
If the backbone is a powerful LLM, what should the \emph{brain around it} look like?
We illustrate the design space with a controlled study whose backbone is
\emph{modeled}, not measured: a fixed-capability oracle that answers iff the needed
fact is in its context --- correctly for simple tasks, and with a fixed per-draft
probability for hard ones --- the \emph{same} capability for every architecture. We
then assemble brains as composite worlds that differ in whether they retrieve from
long-term memory, how many tree-of-thoughts drafts they generate, and whether they
verify them, on a recalled-fact~+~verify suite. \textbf{We stress that the accuracy
ordering below is a consequence of these modeling assumptions, not an empirical
finding}: retrieval makes the rare-fact questions answerable by definition, and
best-of-$N$~+~verify wins the hard questions because several independent draws beat one.
The experiment's purpose is to show that the framework expresses and serializes these
architectures, and to fix the value proposition precisely; the headline numbers are
arithmetic illustrations of it.

Under that modeled backbone (Figure~\ref{fig:brain-arch}, Table~\ref{tab:brainarch}),
the bare LLM scores $\ArchBare$; adding retrieval lifts it to $\ArchMem$; adding
best-of-$N$ with a verifier lifts the hard questions; and the richest brain reaches
$\ArchOpt$ ($+\ArchLift$ absolute over the bare backbone with no change to the LLM),
which a sweep over all $\ArchConfigs$ architectures recovers. The \emph{empirical}
content is engineering, not the lift: the brain serializes to a spec (with a real LLM
emit channel) and round-trips losslessly, and a live Ollama run exercises the genuine
text-in~$\to$~reason~$\to$~text-out loop (\texttt{TextPerceptor}~+~\texttt{LLMEmitter}).
The point the study makes precisely is \emph{with the model frozen, the world model
around it is what you optimize} --- a hypothesis this controlled setup illustrates and
that a real held-constant backbone would be needed to establish.

\subsection{Composition yields better representations (E36)}\label{sec:representations}
E12 and E19 measured what a learner needs to match a \emph{single}
synthesized world; E36 asks the representational question directly on
\emph{factored} worlds, where composition's advantage should appear if it
exists anywhere. The world is an economy of $K$ parametric sectors (the E30
substrate); we sample transitions and learn, pitting a zoo of
\RepNumMono{} monolithic learners on the joint state---linear/ridge
regression, 1-NN and $k$-NN, kernel SVR, a Gaussian process, a random
forest, histogram gradient boosting, a Koopman/EDMD operator on a degree-2
lift, and a two-hidden-layer MLP---against a composite of small per-sector
learners and the exact symbolic composite, on three axes a learned dynamics
model is judged by (Table~\ref{tab:representations},
Figure~\ref{fig:representations}). The comparison is capacity-fair: the
monolith is given at least as many parameters as the composite's children
combined (\RepMonoParams{} vs.\ \RepCompParams{} at $K{=}5$), so any gap is
representational, not a matter of model size.

\emph{Compositional generalization.} Training covers each sector's full
marginal value range but only a thin slice of the joint product; the test
set is the full product, dominated by sector-value \emph{combinations} never
seen together. The composite needs only the marginals---each child sees its
whole small local space---so its accuracy is flat in $K$ (learned-MLP
\RepCompGenLo--\RepCompGenHi; learned-trees and symbolic exact). The entire
monolithic family needs the joint and collapses as the unseen fraction
grows: every one of the \RepNumMono{} learners falls below $0.03$ by $K{=}5$
\emph{except} gradient boosting, the strongest, which still decays from
\RepBestMonoLo{} to \RepBestMonoHi{}---one to two orders of magnitude below
the composite. Crucially, the collapse is not an MLP artifact: it holds
across kernel methods, trees, Gaussian processes, and a Koopman operator
alike, and factoring rescues a \emph{non-MLP} child too---per-sector
gradient boosting reaches \RepCompHgbHi{} where joint gradient boosting
cannot. \emph{Interference.} Trained on the sectors in sequence, the
monolith forgets the first the moment it learns the second (retained
accuracy \RepIntfMono); the composite stores each child separately and
retains \RepIntfComp. \emph{Sample efficiency.} At 100 training transitions
the composite reaches \RepSampCompLo{} against the monolith's
\RepSampMonoLo, and the symbolic composite needs none. The decisive pattern
is a three-tier gradient---monolithic learning collapses (every model class
tried) $\to$ factored learning recovers most of it $\to$ symbolic factoring
is exact and training-free. Factoring the representation, not scaling or
swapping the learner, is what buys generalization here.

\subsection{Adapting to sudden, unannounced rule changes (E41)}\label{sec:nonstationary}
E32 handled a regime switch whose timing was \emph{known}, via a pre-verified
\texttt{PhasedTransition}. E41 asks the harder question a non-stationary world
poses: when the rules change suddenly and \emph{without announcement}, who
notices, and how fast do they recover? A reservoir tracks toward a hidden
target at a hidden rate; that regime jumps at \NonStatChanges{} unannounced
times in a 120-step episode, and at each step the current level is read
through the perception boundary and four predictors forecast the next level
(Figure~\ref{fig:nonstationary}). A symbolic monitor that watches its own
prediction error and re-identifies the rule from the post-change transient
recovers in \NonStatSymLag{} step(s) at each change---snapping back to exact
(\NonStatSymAcc{} overall). A standard sliding-window 1-NN recovers only as its
window refills (\NonStatWinLag{} steps; \NonStatWinAcc{} overall, and only
approximate), and a static model frozen on the first regime never recovers
(\NonStatFrozenAcc), trailed against an oracle that knows the change times
(0-lag, exact). The cumulative-error curves (Figure~\ref{fig:nonstationary}A)
make the difference plain: the symbolic monitor's regret is nearly flat with a
small step at each change, while the frozen model's climbs without bound. Under
noisy perception the ranking is unchanged and every method's error floor rises
by exactly the perception error---the E39 decomposition, now riding on top of
the adaptation dynamics. The mechanism behind the fast recovery is the same one
the paper rests on: because the dynamics are an explicit, identifiable program,
a handful of post-change observations re-pin the rule exactly, where a
statistical model must re-estimate over a window. The boundary is equally
explicit: this holds when the changed rule stays within the identifiable family
the monitor searches; a change to an undeclarable rule admits no exact
recovery.

\subsection{Agents traversing connected worlds with changing rules (E42)}\label{sec:traversal}
E42 is the capstone: it combines composition, toll routes, non-stationary
rules, and per-world perception, and asks whether an agent can track each
world's rule \emph{at the belief level} as it hops between them and the rules
change---including changes that happen while it is away. Two reservoir worlds
are joined by a toll \texttt{Route}; each has its own changing regime and its
own perception boundary; an agent hops every \AgentDwell{} steps
(\AgentHops{} arrivals, \AgentTolls{} tolls paid), and four belief models
predict the current world's next state from the same perceived observations
(Figure~\ref{fig:agent-traversal}). The decisive contrast is between
\emph{separating} a belief per world and \emph{sharing} one. The symbolic
per-world monitor scores \AgentSymAcc{} and, crucially, returns to a world it
left \emph{unchanged} almost for free (recovery \AgentSymUnchg{} steps),
recovering to exact in \AgentSymChanged{} step when the world changed while it
was away. The single shared learner scores \AgentSharedAcc---and pays a
recovery cost of \AgentSharedUnchg{} steps even returning to a world that
\emph{did not change}, because learning the other world overwrote its belief:
catastrophic interference (the E36 result) now induced purely by traversal. A
per-world sliding window avoids interference (\AgentWinAcc) but does not
reliably re-identify an away-change within a single visit. The lesson threads
the whole paper together: separating dynamics into per-world programs---the
composition principle---is what lets an agent carry one world's rule across an
absence intact and re-pin it from a few observations when it has changed, where
an entangled belief cannot hold two worlds at once. The boundary is the
familiar one: exact re-identification holds while the changed rule stays in the
identifiable family.

\subsection{Relativity as a verified world model: reference frames and atomic clocks (E47)}\label{sec:relativity}
Reference frames and changing references are the framework's composition and
non-stationarity story in the language of physics, so E47 encodes special and
general relativity as a verified world model and validates it against real
atomic-clock data. The world's state carries each agent's velocity and proper
time (its atomic clock); the verified transition advances a clock by the
time-dilated amount $\Delta\tau=\Delta t/\gamma$ with
$\gamma=1/\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}$; a \emph{changing reference} is a velocity-change
action and \emph{agent observation} is one frame reading another's clock through
the Lorentz transform (Figure~\ref{fig:relativity}).

Four results. (i) \emph{Time dilation} is exact for the symbolic model at every
speed, whereas a learned fit on low-speed data diverges as $v\to c$ (error
\RelLearnErr{} of a clock tick at $0.999c$) and the Newtonian ``no dilation''
model is off by \RelNewtErr---the classical physics that real clocks disprove.
(ii) Relativistic velocity addition stays below $c$ while the Galilean sum runs
superluminal. (iii) The \emph{twin paradox}, simulated as an agent worldline with
a turnaround, has the traveler return \RelTwinDiff{} years younger
(\RelTwinTravel{} vs.\ \RelTwinStay{}) while the frame-naive model ages both
identically---the asymmetry is entirely the change of reference. (iv) Most
tellingly, the same verified model reproduces \emph{real measurements}: from
first principles the GPS clock correction is $\RelGpsSR$ (SR) $+\,\RelGpsGR$ (GR)
$=\RelGpsNet$~$\mu$s/day, matching the documented $\sim$+38; and at
flight-averaged parameters the Hafele--Keating shifts come out eastward
\RelHKModelEast~ns and westward \RelHKModelWest~ns, inside the 1971 measured
$\RelHKObsEast$~ns and $\RelHKObsWest$~ns (the east/west asymmetry exists only
because of relativity in the rotating frame; the Newtonian model predicts zero).
A verified-code world model, declared from textbook physics, is exact in the
relativistic regime and lands inside the error bars of a real atomic-clock
experiment---the same exact-vs-approximate story as the rest of the paper, now in
a domain where the ``approximate'' (classical) model is known to fail and is
corrected for daily in orbit.

\subsection{A same-day trading world model: out-of-sample on real markets (E50)}\label{sec:trading}
E50 applies the framework to a deliberately hard, near-efficient real-world
problem: from \TradeUniverse{} large-cap tickers' history (real OHLC via
yfinance, \TradeYears{} years), rank the ticker most likely to rise and recommend
the same-day entry---buy at the open (capture the intraday segment, close→open
$\to$ open→close) or at the close (capture the overnight segment). Each ticker is
a world with verified, auditable estimators of P(up) per segment; selection is a
many-worlds pick of the highest expected move; timing is the open/close choice.
The value here is not alpha---it is \emph{rigor}: an exact, auditable model and an
walk-forward, no-lookahead, after-cost backtest that makes overfitting
visible, where a black-box predictor would hide it.

The result is reported as-is (Figure~\ref{fig:trading}, Table~\ref{tab:trading}).
On a synthetic market with a \emph{known} $+40$\,bps overnight edge the detector
recovers it cleanly (Sharpe \TradeSynSharpe{} vs.\ \TradeSynRandom{} random), so a
weak real result reads as ``market efficient'', not ``detector broken.'' On real
data the out-of-sample strategy returns \TradeHonestAnn\% annualized after
cost---nominally above buy-and-hold SPY (\TradeSpyAnn\%)---but its hit rate is
only \TradeHonestHit\%, its risk-adjusted return is \emph{below} SPY (Sharpe
\TradeHonestSharpe{} vs.\ \TradeSpySharpe{}), and the edge is cost-fragile: the
no-cost Sharpe is \TradeNoCostSharpe{} (close to the lookahead-cheating
\TradeLookaheadSharpe{}) and the annualized return crosses zero around
\TradeCostBreakeven\,bps round-trip. In other words, most of the apparent edge is
transaction cost and overfitting; what survives does not beat simply holding the
index on a risk-adjusted basis. That verdict---and the auditable,
cost-swept, no-lookahead protocol that produces it---is the contribution; the
model also emits a concrete daily recommendation (ticker + open/close entry) for
live forward-testing, with the obvious caveat that this is not investment advice.

\subsection{Wavelet denoising as a perception$\to$world$\to$emit loop (E52)}\label{sec:denoise}
A wavelet denoiser is a clean instance of the perception boundary: a noisy
continuous signal is mapped to a \emph{sparse symbolic state} (the few
significant wavelet coefficients --- real structure separated from dense noise),
a declared, auditable rule shrinks the noise coefficients (soft-threshold at the
universal $\lambda=\sigma\sqrt{2\ln N}$, $\sigma$ the robust MAD estimate), and
the inverse transform emits the cleaned signal. We implement the transform in
numpy (orthonormal Haar, bit-exact perfect reconstruction --- max round-trip
error \DenoisePR{}, a built-in invariant), with the multi-resolution
decomposition as a composite of scale sub-bands. On noisy input the symbolic
state is \DenoiseSparsity\% zero (the compactness the threshold buys).

The result is a basis-match story (Figure~\ref{fig:denoise},
Table~\ref{tab:denoise}). On \emph{discontinuous} signals (Donoho's blocks and
heavisine), where the wavelet basis matches the structure, the parameter-free
wavelet denoiser gains \DenoiseEdgeWavelet\,dB SNR --- beating even an
\emph{optimally tuned} low-pass (\DenoiseEdgeTuned\,dB) and a naive one
(\DenoiseEdgeNaive\,dB), because Fourier filtering blurs and rings the jumps the
wavelet preserves. It is also parameter-free: the low-pass must be hand-tuned to
rise from \DenoiseNaiveMean{} to \DenoiseTunedMean\,dB, while the wavelet's
threshold is automatic. We report the mismatches rather than hide them: on
\emph{smooth/oscillatory} signals (bumps, doppler) a Fourier low-pass matches the
structure better (\DenoiseSmoothTuned{} vs.\ \DenoiseSmoothWavelet\,dB), and on
\emph{harmonic speech} the Haar basis is simply wrong (\DenoiseSpeech\,dB ---
harmonics are sparse in Fourier, not Haar; a smoother db/sym or learned basis is
needed). The lesson is the framework's, in signal form: a verified, auditable,
training-free world model wins precisely where its declared structure matches the
signal's, and the experiment makes that boundary visible. The model emits
before/after \texttt{.wav} files (blocks, doppler, speech) to listen to.

\subsection{Abstract interpretation: sound, tight bounds on rollouts (E54)}\label{sec:bounds}
A verified world model can be run not just on concrete states but on
\emph{abstract} ones, propagating a whole set of possible states forward at once
and returning guaranteed bounds on where the world can go. That is abstract
interpretation. The naive abstraction---intervals---is sound but loses all
correlation between variables, so bounds blow up under feedback. We add an affine domain that carries shared noise symbols, so cancellations like
$a + (1-a)$ are tracked exactly, and test both on a recurring contractive update
$x_{t+1}=a\,x_t+(1-a)c+\text{shock}$ with $a$ ranging in $[0.70,0.95]$ each step.

Over a \BoundsHorizon-step horizon (Figure~\ref{fig:bounds},
Table~\ref{tab:bounds}) the affine bound stays tight---width \BoundsAffineWidth{}
against the true reachable width \BoundsTrueWidth---while the interval bound
explodes to \BoundsIntervalWidth{} by losing the $a/(1-a)$ correlation. Both
abstract methods are \emph{sound} (they always enclose the truth); a Monte-Carlo
envelope of the same system is tighter but \emph{unsound}, missing the true
maximum by up to \BoundsMcMiss{} units---the failure mode that matters when the
bound is a safety guarantee. Abstract interpretation gives the world model a
``worst-case rollout'' it can actually certify.

\subsection{Information geometry: identify the world by maximizing information (E55)}\label{sec:infogeom}
When the true world is one of $K$ candidates and each probe yields a noisy,
world-dependent outcome, the right next experiment is the one whose outcome
carries the most information about which world is true---the expected information
gain (mutual information between outcome and world identity under the current
posterior), the information-geometric / optimal-experiment criterion. This
sharpens the active-induction story (companion paper~\cite{openworld_wtc}) and the
many-worlds posterior (\S\ref{sec:many-worlds}): choose probes by information,
update by Bayes. We compare it to random probing and to the count-based
version-space heuristic on \InfoGeomWorlds{} candidate worlds and \InfoGeomProbes{}
probes.

Expected information gain identifies the true world in \InfoGeomEig{} probes on
average, against \InfoGeomHeuristic{} for the version-space heuristic and
\InfoGeomRandom{} for random probing (Figure~\ref{fig:infogeom},
Table~\ref{tab:infogeom}), at essentially perfect accuracy. The reason is
adaptivity: as the posterior concentrates, the criterion stops re-confirming what
is already known and picks probes that separate the worlds still in
contention---something the probability-blind count heuristic cannot do.

\subsection{Optimal transport: drift detection and calibration where KL saturates (E56)}\label{sec:transport}
Comparing a world's output distribution to data is usually done with KL
divergence, but KL is uninformative in exactly the regime drift detection cares about: under a regime shift the new
distribution moves off the old one, supports stop overlapping, and KL saturates to
an uninformative plateau (or diverges)---it cannot say \emph{how far} the world
drifted, only that it did. The Wasserstein (earth-mover) distance instead measures
how far probability mass must move, so it stays finite, scales with the size of
the shift, and gives a usable gradient even across disjoint supports. We use the
closed-form 1-D Wasserstein (the $L_1$ distance between quantile functions) for
scalar world outputs.

On a four-regime stream the consecutive-window Wasserstein distance spikes at
each shift and localizes both true change-points (\TransportTrueShifts{}) exactly
(Figure~\ref{fig:transport}, Table~\ref{tab:transport}). The sharper result is
calibration: fitting a world's mean to data from a cold start whose support does
not overlap the target ($\mu^*=8$), minimizing Wasserstein recovers
$\hat\mu=\TransportMuHat$ because the objective slopes toward the target
everywhere (far-region relative slope \TransportWSlope), whereas the KL objective
is flat there (slope \TransportKlSlope)---no descent direction at all. Optimal
transport is the distance that lets a world model be \emph{calibrated and
monitored} across the very shifts where KL goes blind.

\section{Reproducibility}

\texttt{experiments/} write \texttt{results/*.json} (seeds fixed in-source);
\texttt{python3 scripts/make\_paper\_assets.py} regenerates every figure,
