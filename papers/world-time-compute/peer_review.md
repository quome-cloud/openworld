## Summary

The paper makes two claims and defends them with two different evidentiary standards. The first â€” that LLM-synthesized, verified code dynamics are bit-exact and out-of-distribution-robust where per-step LLM prediction and trained MLP/1-NN baselines compound error and collapse â€” is measured deterministically (exact-match against ground-truth oracles) and is airtight; every headline number in this half of the paper (Tables `main`, `fidelity`, `induction`, `ladder`, `bakeoff`, `planning`) reproduces exactly from the cited JSON. The second, larger claim â€” "world-time compute": fine-tuning on trajectories drawn from many verified worlds raises held-out generalization, and the lift specifically requires label *exactness* (shown via a corrupted-label control repeated across essentially every experiment in this half) â€” is the paper's real contribution, and the corrupted-label discipline applied uniformly across diagnosis worlds, coding worlds, ARC-AGI, List Functions, CLRS-Text, Bongard-RWR, and cross-world transfer is a genuinely strong, consistent causal design. But several of the *scaling*- and *curve-shape* claims built on top of that design â€” the world-count scaling curve, the cross-world "worlds traversed" ladder, and the ARC cross-task ladder â€” rest on single-seed or two-seed point estimates that the prose describes with more confidence ("no plateau," "peaking... then leveling off") than the underlying numbers support; in one case (the E84 cross-world ladder) the described shape ("leveling off") does not match the actual data (a *decline* from the 64-world peak to the 128-world point). Separately, one of the two claimed differentiators from prior self-training work â€” that training on a full verified *trajectory* beats training on a final *answer* â€” is itself shown by the paper's own E85 ablation to reverse at longer rollout horizons, a reversal the text undersells as merely "catches up." The related-work section is thorough on the code-world-model and self-training lineages but omits the model-based-RL tradition (Dyna-style training on simulator-generated experience) that is arguably the closest conceptual ancestor of "world-time compute" itself. None of this threatens the deterministic-exactness half of the paper, which is genuinely publication-ready; it does mean the generalization-lever half needs a revision pass on statistical hedging and framing before its scaling claims should be taken at face value. **Verdict: Major Revision** â€” the core causal claim survives, but several secondary scaling/curve-shape claims and one differentiator claim need to be re-hedged or re-measured, and a literature gap should be closed.

## Strengths

- **A genuinely disciplined, repeated causal design.** The corrupted-label ablation (randomize training labels, hold everything else fixed) is not a one-off control; it is applied as the same discriminator across at least seven independent settings â€” diagnosis worlds (E78b), the coding world family (E77 note), real ARC-AGI (E80, Fig. `arc`), List Functions, CLRS-Text, and Bongard-RWR (all in Fig. `worldtime-domains`), cross-world transfer on List Functions (E84, Fig. `crossworld`), and the language-frame composite (E81) â€” and it is the one piece of evidence load-bearing enough to answer the paper's own posed question ("is the verified qualifier load-bearing, or decoration on plain SFT?", Â§Discussion, line 1420). Independently recomputing `e78b_ablation.json`'s `summary` confirms `AblVerified`=35.4%, `AblNoisyFull`=18.75%â‰ˆ19%, and `AblFloorHard`=22.9%â‰ˆ23% all match the paper's macros exactly.
- **Honest negative results treated as first-class findings, not buried.** Bongard-RWR (vision) stays flat at chance with no scaling and no collapse under corruption (Â§4.5, `BongBase`=46%â†’`BongHeavy`=48%â†’`BongCorrupt`=52%, i.e. corruption does *not* hurt â€” the cleanest possible null); the language-frame composite (E81, Â§4.6) reports a clean negative on *both* base models, including a case where the "headroom hypothesis predicted the largest gain" and instead the weak model got worse; the CLRS cross-world null (E83, Â§4.8) is reported "as a weak null" with the honest caveat that the base already sits near floor, leaving little room to move. These are exactly the self-deflating, mechanism-clarifying negatives that make the positive results credible.
- **Reproducibility machinery above field norm.** A CI workflow regenerates `numbers.tex` from committed result JSON and fails the build if it would change (Â§Reproducibility, line 1797-1800); `experiments/MANIFEST.md` classifies every experiment as deterministic-offline vs. Ollama-dependent; model snapshots and quantizations are pinned per result file. This is verifiable, not merely claimed â€” every macro I spot-checked against its source JSON matched.
- **Mechanism-isolating experiment sequencing (E37â†’E38â†’E43).** E37 removes the information asymmetry between the synthesizer (given rules) and the learned baselines (given only traces); E38 asks whether the resulting induction ceiling is a capability limit and shows it is flat across three model families/scales, ruling that out; E43 then shows the real bottleneck is *identifiability* by demonstrating an active-probing policy closes the gap that no amount of passive data or bigger models closed. This three-experiment arc is a model of how to chase down a "why" rather than stopping at a single ablation.
- **A limitations section that pre-empts real objections rather than hedging generically.** Fourteen numbered items (Â§Discussion) explicitly flag: the rule-text vs. trace-induction asymmetry in the headline comparison (i), the complexity cliff boundary (ii), no multiplicity correction across many reported p-values with an explicit list of which borderline results that implicates (x), and single-seed status for several stochastic headline quantities including the E74 model-size sweep (xi). This is unusually candid for the genre.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **(Major) The E85 "trajectory unit" advantage â€” one of the paper's two claimed differentiators from prior self-training work â€” reverses at longer horizons, and the text undersells the reversal as merely "catching up."** Â§Discussion (line 1446-1455) reports: at the trained horizon, `TrajTrainTraj`=70% beats `TrajTrainAnswer`=58% (+12 pts, the claimed advantage of training on trajectories rather than answers). But rolled out to a horizon *longer* than trained, `TrajLongAnswer`=75% actually exceeds `TrajLongTraj`=68% â€” the answer-unit does not merely "catch up," it overtakes by 7 points. The paper's own sentence names the mechanism ("the model's per-step predictions compound, the very error mode verified code avoids") but applies it only to explain away the trajectory unit's *relative* decline, not to flag that the ranking has flipped. This matters because the Discussion explicitly uses the trajectory-vs-answer distinction as one of "two things [that] earn the approach its name over 'SFT with extra steps'" (line 1427-1429) â€” a claim that is only true within the trained horizon and reverses at extrapolated horizons, which is precisely the regime (iv) of the Limitations section flags as "future work" without connecting it back to this result. *Fix:* state the reversal plainly and qualify the trajectory-unit claim as horizon-bounded, or drop it from the "what earns the name" argument until the reversal is itself explained.

2. **(Major) The E84 cross-world "worlds traversed" ladder is described as "leveling off" when the underlying single-seed data show a decline from the peak.** Â§Crossworld (line 964-967) and Fig. `crossworld_ladder`'s caption state the lift "grows... from `CrossWorldLadderLo` at `CrossWorldLadderLoN` worlds to a `CrossWorldLadderHi` peak by 64 worlds, then leveling off" and the caption reads "More verified worlds â†’ more transfer." The source data (`e84_crossworld_listfn_ladder.json`) show: N=16â†’29.2%, N=32â†’43.9%, N=64â†’48.9% (the stated peak), **N=128â†’45.8%** â€” a 3.1-point *decline* from the 64-world point to the 128-world point, not a plateau. This is exactly the point range where the paper's own 3-seed headline result sits (Fig. `crossworld`, 128 held-in worlds, `CrossWorldMean`=40%), so the single-seed ladder disagrees in direction with the multi-seed headline at the same N. The figure caption does disclose "(Single-seed ladder...)" but the body-text sentence built on top of it ("rises then plateaus," "more worlds â†’ more transfer") does not repeat that hedge, and "leveling off" is a materially different claim from "declined by 3 points on the one seed we ran." *Fix:* either report seeded CIs at each ladder rung, or rewrite the sentence to state a decline (with the appropriate noise caveat) rather than a plateau.

3. **(Major) Several headline scaling/curve-shape claims rest on single- or two-seed measurements while the prose states them with unqualified confidence.** This is a pattern across three places: (a) E76's 7B world-count curve (Fig. `world_count`, `WCMaxAcc`=59% at `WCMaxWorlds`=512) is single-seed per `e76_world_count.json` (no `seeds`/`ci` fields), yet the text asserts it is "*still climbing* at 512 worlds... no plateau" (line 741) as a load-bearing claim for "world-time compute is a genuine scaling lever." (b) The ARC cross-task ladder (`e80_arc_ladder.json`, `n_eval`=100, single seed) is non-monotonic â€” rungs at Nâˆˆ{25,50,100,200} score {6%, 5%, 3%, 5%} â€” yet the paper cites only the endpoint pair (`ArcLadderBase`=3% base â†’ `ArcLadderTop`=5% at N=200) and omits that the *fewest*-world rung (N=25) scored *highest* (6%), a fact that, if within Wilson noise at n=100 (plausible), undercuts using any single rung as evidence either way. (c) The primary ARC test-time-training result (Â§4.4) is itself single-seed (`e80_arc_ttt.json`); the 2-seed confirmatory rerun (`ArcSeedsN`=2, `e80_arc_ttt_seeds.json`) is a good-faith attempt to add variance information but is still thin (n=2) for the CI claimed in the abstract (`ArcHeavyCI`=[2,20]). Points (a)-(c) are honestly disclosed in aggregate in Limitations item (xi) ("the E74 model-size scaling is a single fine-tuning seed per size, and the real-data ARC/CLRS arms are single-seed... should be read as exploratory"), but that hedge is not repeated at each individual point of use in the Results section, where the language ("no plateau," "still climbing," "grows... with compute") reads as a settled trend rather than an exploratory one. *Fix:* either add seeds to the two single-run curves that carry rhetorical weight (E76's 7B sweep, the ARC ladder), which the paper's own infrastructure clearly supports (E78 already re-runs an analogous sweep with 5 seeds for smaller models), or soften the curve-shape language to match the n=1 status inline, not only in the aggregated limitations paragraph.

4. **(Major) Missing citation to the model-based-RL / Dyna lineage weakens the novelty framing.** The paper carefully positions itself against the programmatic code-world-model literature (WorldCoder, PoE-World, GIF-MCTS, CWM-games) and against the self-training lineage (STaR, ReST/ReST-EM, expert iteration, RFT, RLVR, TTRL) (Â§Introduction, "Positioning in the field," lines 402-434), arguing its differentiator is "what does the verifying (a full world model with dynamics...)" and "the generalization axis (held-out worlds in a family)." But the paper's actual mechanism â€” train a model on trajectories generated by rolling a (here: verified) simulator, rather than on real environment data â€” is the founding idea of Sutton's Dyna architecture and is exactly what Dreamer's own "imagination" training does (Dreamer is cited repeatedly in this paper, including in the direct DreamerV3 comparisons of Â§4.9/Â§4.10, but only for its *architecture*, never for the "train the policy/model on simulated experience" idea it shares with world-time compute). A `grep` of `refs.bib` and `main.tex` for "Dyna" and for the broader synthetic-training-data literature (Self-Instruct, textbooks-are-all-you-need-style corpora, Evol-Instruct) returns nothing. Given the paper's stated problem framing â€” "real, labeled examples are scarce... we study a cheap way to make them" (Abstract, line 163) â€” this is precisely the space these lines of work occupy, and their absence makes the novelty claim ("no general tool has made authoring the many verified world models this needs cheap," line 331) read as narrower than it should: the *training-on-simulated-data* half of the contribution has real precedent that isn't engaged with; only the *verified-code-simulator* half is genuinely differentiated from it. *Fix:* add Dyna/model-based-RL citations and a paragraph distinguishing world-time compute from "train on simulator rollouts" as done in Dyna/Dreamer (the natural answer is exactness + auditability of the simulator, which the paper already argues elsewhere, but it needs to be said against this specific prior art, not just against self-training).

## Detailed Technical Questions

**Q1.** For the E84 cross-world ladder (Fig. `crossworld_ladder`), can you provide per-rung multi-seed CIs (16/32/64/128 held-in worlds), not just the 3-seed headline at 128? Does the 64â†’128 decline (48.9%â†’45.8%) persist with more seeds, or is it noise? This directly decides whether "leveling off" or "declining after a peak near 64" is the correct description, and whether the paper's advice to practitioners ("more verified worlds â†’ more transfer") should instead say "up to roughly 64, on this domain."

**Q2.** For E85, do you have intermediate-horizon data between the trained horizon and the "longer" test horizon, to locate where the answer-unit overtakes the trajectory-unit, and is that crossover stable across the 3 seeds (`TrajNSeeds`)? Should the Discussion's claim "the trajectory is the better training signal" (line 1454) be restated as horizon-bounded?

**Q3.** E76's world-count scaling curve is single-seed for the 7B model on the hard family, while E78 runs an essentially parallel sweep with 5 seeds for the 0.5B/1.5B ("small"/"med") models. Given that infrastructure already exists, why wasn't the 7B curve (the one used for the headline "no plateau" claim) given the same multi-seed treatment, and does the "still climbing at 512 worlds" conclusion survive averaging?

**Q4.** For the ARC cross-task ladder (Â§4.4(i)), the N=25 rung scores highest (6%) among all five points despite having the fewest training worlds. Is this within the Wilson band at n_eval=100, and if the whole curve is statistically indistinguishable from flat, should the paper report that explicitly rather than citing the baseâ†’N=200 endpoint pair as evidence of (weak) improvement?

**Q5.** How does world-time compute relate to Dyna-style training on simulator-generated experience and to Dreamer's use of imagined rollouts as training signal (both trained *policies* on model-generated trajectories rather than real environment data)? Is the differentiator solely that the simulator here is verified/exact, or is there a deeper mechanistic difference (e.g., the trajectory-as-training-unit point in E85, modulo Weakness 1)?

## Overall Assessment

**Recommendation:** Major Revision
**Confidence:** 4

The paper's core deterministic claim â€” verified, synthesized code dynamics are bit-exact and OOD-robust where per-step LLM prediction and trained MLP/1-NN baselines compound error and collapse â€” is measured cleanly, reproduces exactly against its own JSON, and is not in question; it, along with the planning and bake-off results built on it, is ready to publish as written. The paper's second, larger claim â€” that world-time compute's held-out generalization lift specifically requires label exactness, not mere task variety â€” is backed by an unusually disciplined corrupted-label ablation repeated across seven-plus independent domains, and that causal core also survives scrutiny. What currently outruns the evidence is the rhetorical layer built on top: curve-shape language ("no plateau," "leveling off," "grows with compute") applied to single- or two-seed sweeps at exactly the places (E76's 7B world-count curve, the E84 cross-world ladder, the ARC cross-task ladder) where the paper's own multi-seed infrastructure (visible in E78, in the 3-seed E84 headline, in the 2-seed ARC rerun) shows it knows how to do better, and one claimed differentiator (trajectory-unit training) that the paper's own E85 data show reverses beyond the trained horizon without the text flagging the reversal. A revision set that would move this to Accept: (a) add seeds (or explicitly re-hedge inline, not just in the aggregated Limitations paragraph) for the E76 and ARC-ladder curves, and report per-rung CIs for the E84 ladder; (b) rewrite the E85 paragraph to state the answer-unit's long-horizon reversal plainly and qualify the trajectory-unit claim as horizon-bounded; (c) add Dyna / model-based-RL and synthetic-training-data citations and situate the contribution against them explicitly; (d) fix the "each gate buys +0.24" wording. None of these revisions touch the paper's strongest, most novel evidence (the corrupted-label discriminator applied at scale across real and synthetic domains), which is publication-worthy as is.
